// 01 — Why MDCG 2025-6 Matters

The EU Dual Compliance Challenge for AI Medical Devices

From August 2, 2026, AI-enabled medical devices placed on the EU market must comply with two distinct regulatory frameworks simultaneously: EU MDR 2017/745 (medical device safety and performance) and the EU AI Act 2024/1689 (artificial intelligence systems safety and transparency). MDCG 2025-6 is the European Commission Medical Device Coordination Group's guidance document specifically addressing how these two frameworks interact for medical devices.

MDCG 2025-6 does not reduce the obligation of either framework — it clarifies how to navigate them together. The core message: MDR compliance does not imply AI Act compliance, and vice versa. Manufacturers must build a compliance programme that explicitly addresses both.

Status of MDCG 2025-6

MDCG 2025-6 "Guidance on the Applicability of the AI Regulation to Medical Devices and In Vitro Diagnostic Medical Devices" represents the consensus position of EU Competent Authorities. It is non-binding but authoritative — Notified Bodies reviewing AI medical device Technical Documentation will assess conformity against its framework. Manufacturers deviating from its guidance without documented justification face significant audit risk.

// 02 — AI Act Scope for Medical Devices

Does the EU AI Act Apply to Your Device?

The EU AI Act applies to AI systems — not all software qualifies. The Act defines an AI system as: machine-based system designed to operate with varying levels of autonomy, that may exhibit adaptiveness after deployment, and that infers from inputs how to generate outputs such as predictions, content, recommendations, or decisions influencing real or virtual environments. Classic rule-based algorithms and simple lookup tables are not AI systems. Machine learning models — including CNN, transformer, and tree-based models — clearly are.

High-Risk AI — Annex III Classification

AI medical devices are presumed high-risk under EU AI Act Annex III, Category 5(a): AI systems intended to be used as safety components of products covered by Union harmonisation legislation listed in Annex I (which includes MDR and IVDR). This means virtually all AI-enabled medical devices that qualify as medical devices under MDR are automatically classified as high-risk AI under the AI Act. There is no equivalent of MDR's Class I — all are high-risk AI.

General Purpose AI (GPAI) Models

If your medical device incorporates a General Purpose AI model (e.g. a large language model as a component), additional AI Act obligations apply to the GPAI model provider. MDCG 2025-6 clarifies that the medical device manufacturer is responsible for ensuring GPAI components used in their device comply with AI Act requirements — even if the GPAI model itself was developed by a third party. This creates a supply chain obligation analogous to SOUP management in IEC 62304.

// 04 — Practical Implementation

Building One Integrated Compliance Programme

1

Map Your AI Components

Identify every AI/ML component in your device: which are AI systems under the AI Act definition, which are GPAI components from third parties, which are classical algorithms (not AI Act scope). Document this mapping in your Technical Documentation. Third-party GPAI components require due diligence: obtain the GPAI provider's AI Act technical documentation and verify compliance obligations are allocated correctly in your supply agreements.

2

Unified Technical Documentation Architecture

Structure your Technical Documentation with a master index that maps to both MDR Annex II sections and AI Act Annex IV sections. Each section of the Technical Documentation should explicitly state which regulation it addresses. Regulators — both Notified Bodies under MDR and market surveillance authorities under the AI Act — will assess conformity against this structure.

3

Integrated Risk Management

Extend your ISO 14971 risk management process to explicitly address AI-specific risks: model failures, dataset bias, distribution shift, adversarial inputs, and explainability gaps. These risks must be documented in your risk management file with associated risk controls. The AI Act's Article 9 risk management obligations are satisfied by an ISO 14971 process that explicitly covers AI-specific hazards — but only if the process is documented as such.

4

AI Transparency Obligations in IFU

The AI Act requires users be informed of the AI system's purpose, capabilities, limitations, accuracy metrics, and human oversight requirements. This goes substantially beyond typical MDR IFU content. Add a dedicated AI Transparency section to your IFU covering: what the AI detects/predicts, validated performance metrics (sensitivity, specificity, subgroup performance), known limitations and failure modes, how to override or disregard AI output, and training requirements for operators. Draft this section early — it requires clinical and regulatory input from multiple teams.

5

Dual NB Strategy

The AI Act requires Notified Body conformity assessment for high-risk AI systems — but the NB designated under the AI Act may be different from the NB designated under MDR. MDCG 2025-6 strongly encourages manufacturers to select a single NB with dual designation under both MDR and the AI Act where possible. This eliminates the risk of conflicting technical opinions between two separate NBs assessing the same device. Check your NB's AI Act designation status now — many have not yet received it.

// 05 — Summary

Key Takeaways

MDR ≠ AI Act Compliance

These are two separate legal frameworks. CE marking under MDR does not satisfy AI Act obligations. Both must be explicitly addressed in your Technical Documentation, risk management file, IFU, and post-market monitoring programme.

All AI Medical Devices Are High-Risk AI

Under EU AI Act Annex III Category 5(a), virtually all AI-enabled medical devices are high-risk AI systems. There is no Class I equivalent in the AI Act for medical AI. Every AI medical device requires a full high-risk AI compliance programme.

Human Oversight Must Be Designed In

AI Act Article 14 requires that operators can understand, monitor, and override AI outputs. Design override mechanisms into your device from the start — adding them later is architecturally complex and commercially disruptive. Document human oversight measures in both the Technical Documentation and the IFU.

August 2026 Deadline Is Real

AI Act high-risk AI obligations apply from August 2, 2026. Devices placing on the EU market after that date must comply. For devices in development now, compliance must be built in — not added retroactively. MDCG 2025-6 provides the map; follow it now.

Next Step Recommendation

Immediately: (1) Confirm whether your device contains AI systems under the AI Act definition. (2) Check your Notified Body's AI Act designation status. (3) Audit your Technical Documentation against AI Act Annex IV requirements — the gaps define your compliance work programme. (4) Add AI Act obligations to your risk management procedure and IFU template. Module 04 covers bias analysis, which is a mandatory component of both MDR GMLP compliance and AI Act Article 10 data governance.
