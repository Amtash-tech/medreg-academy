// 01 — Foundation

Why Bias in Medical AI Is a Patient Safety Issue

Algorithmic bias in medical AI is not merely a fairness concern — it is a patient safety issue with direct regulatory consequences. An AI diagnostic tool that performs 15% worse in dark-skinned patients than in light-skinned patients will cause disproportionate misdiagnosis in one population. A sepsis alert that is systematically under-sensitive in elderly women will result in delayed treatment and preventable deaths.

FDA, through its GMLP principles and AI/ML Action Plan, explicitly requires manufacturers to examine bias in their AI/ML devices. EU AI Act Article 10 mandates data governance practices that include bias examination. Both frameworks treat bias not as an academic concern but as a mandatory component of the device's safety case.

Regulatory Foundation

GMLP Principle 6: "Manage the impact of dataset and algorithmic bias." EU AI Act Article 10(f): training, validation, and test data must be "examined in view of possible biases." FDA AI/ML Action Plan (2021): bias assessment is a key pillar of the framework for AI/ML-based SaMD oversight. MDCG 2025-6 reinforces bias analysis as an AI Act Article 10 obligation for all high-risk AI medical devices.

Types of Bias in Medical AI: A Regulatory Taxonomy

Regulators and manufacturers must distinguish between different sources of bias because each requires a different mitigation strategy:

// 03 — Analysis Methodology

Performing a Regulatory-Grade Bias Analysis

1

Define Subgroups Before Training

Identify, in your study protocol written before any model training, all subgroups you will analyse for disparate performance. At minimum: age bands (e.g. 18-40, 41-60, 61-75, 75+), biological sex, race/ethnicity (aligned to US Census or equivalent population categories), disease severity strata, and clinical site. Pre-specification is essential — post-hoc subgroup selection that happens to show favourable results is a methodological flaw that FDA reviewers are trained to identify.

2

Ensure Subgroup Sample Size Adequacy

Subgroup analysis requires adequate sample sizes for each subgroup to draw meaningful conclusions. As a rule of thumb, each subgroup should have sufficient events to estimate performance metrics with confidence intervals that are clinically interpretable. A subgroup of n=12 with sensitivity=0.83 (95% CI: 0.52–0.98) is uninformative — the confidence interval spans the difference between acceptable and unacceptable performance. Power calculations for subgroup analysis should be pre-specified.

3

Calculate Fairness Metrics Across Subgroups

Report primary performance metrics (sensitivity, specificity, AUC, PPV, NPV) stratified by each pre-specified subgroup. Calculate disparity metrics: Equalised Odds (does the model have equal true positive rates and false positive rates across subgroups?); Demographic Parity (is the positive prediction rate equal across subgroups?); Calibration by subgroup (are confidence scores reliable across all subgroups?). No single fairness metric is universally correct — choose metrics appropriate to the clinical context and justify your choice.

4

Investigate Root Causes of Observed Disparities

When performance disparities are detected, investigate root causes before selecting mitigation strategies: Is the disparity due to dataset representation bias? Label bias? Measurement bias? Inherent clinical heterogeneity? The root cause determines the mitigation. Adding more data from the underperforming subgroup (addressing representation bias) will not fix a label bias problem that affects the new data equally.

5

Apply and Validate Mitigations

Mitigation options by bias type: Representation bias: targeted data collection, oversampling, SMOTE, weighted loss functions. Label bias: subgroup-stratified annotation quality review, expert panel adjudication for underperforming subgroups. Architecture bias: fairness-constrained training, subgroup-specific model calibration, ensemble approaches. After applying mitigations, re-evaluate on the test set — document the before-and-after performance for each subgroup to demonstrate the mitigation was effective.

// 04 — Disclosure Obligations

What Regulators Expect You to Disclose

In the FDA Submission

Full subgroup performance table for all pre-specified subgroups. Documentation of bias analysis methodology and mitigation strategies applied. Discussion of residual performance disparities that could not be fully mitigated. Labelling of the performance table with subgroup-level confidence intervals. For any subgroup where performance is substantially worse, explicit acknowledgement and proposed labelling language limiting use or adding warnings.

In the IFU / Device Labelling

Subgroup-level performance metrics — not just overall performance — must appear in the device labelling for cleared AI devices. If performance is substantially worse in a specific subgroup (e.g. age >85, BMI >40), this must be stated as a limitation and/or contraindication. AI Act Article 13 requires that the instructions for use explicitly disclose performance metrics and limitations — including subgroup performance disparities.

Post-Market Bias Monitoring

Bias analysis is not a one-time pre-market activity. Post-market, you must monitor for performance disparities across subgroups in the real-world deployed population — which may differ from your test set. Define thresholds in your PMS plan: if subgroup performance drops below a specified level, it triggers investigation and potentially a PCCP-covered retraining or a new submission.

Unresolvable Disparities: What to Do

If bias analysis reveals a performance disparity that cannot be adequately mitigated (e.g. due to fundamental measurement bias or insufficient data for rare subgroups), you have three options: (1) add a contraindication excluding the underperforming subgroup from intended use; (2) add a warning requiring additional clinical assessment for that subgroup; (3) restrict the intended use population to those in whom adequate performance is demonstrated. Option 3 is the most defensible — narrowing your intended use rather than claiming performance you cannot demonstrate.

// 05 — Summary

Key Takeaways

Pre-Specify Subgroups Before Training

Define your subgroup analysis plan in writing before any model training. Post-hoc subgroup selection — choosing to report only the subgroups where your device performs well — is a methodological flaw that experienced FDA reviewers will identify and challenge.

Root Cause Determines Mitigation

Bias types have different root causes and require different mitigations. Adding more data fixes representation bias — but not label bias or measurement bias. Investigate before treating. Applying the wrong mitigation wastes time and may not improve performance in the subgroup where patients are actually being harmed.

Disclose What You Find

Regulators do not expect perfect bias-free performance — they expect honest analysis and appropriate disclosure. A submission that demonstrates rigorous bias analysis, documents residual disparities, and proposes appropriate labelling limitations is far stronger than one that glosses over subgroup performance. Concealing performance disparities is a regulatory integrity issue.

Monitor Bias Post-Market

Real-world populations differ from test populations. Pre-market bias analysis is necessary but not sufficient. Build subgroup performance monitoring into your post-market surveillance programme — with defined thresholds that trigger investigation and response.

Next Step Recommendation

Write your subgroup analysis plan as part of your dataset design document — before collecting any data. Identify your target subgroups, specify sample size requirements for each, and pre-specify the performance thresholds you will apply. Then integrate bias monitoring metrics into your PCCP Impact Assessment Protocol (Module 01) and your post-market surveillance plan (Module 06).
