// 01 — Foundation

What GMLP Is and Why It Exists

Good Machine Learning Practice (GMLP) is a set of 10 guiding principles developed jointly by FDA, Health Canada, and the UK MHRA, published in October 2021. It represents the first international regulatory consensus on what constitutes responsible AI/ML medical device development — analogous to GCP (Good Clinical Practice) for clinical trials or GMP (Good Manufacturing Practice) for device manufacturing.

GMLP does not specify technical implementation — it specifies principles that development programmes must embody. Regulators evaluate whether a manufacturer's processes, documentation, and validation evidence demonstrate adherence to these principles. A submission that clearly maps to GMLP principles is structured in a way FDA, Health Canada, and MHRA reviewers find immediately legible — this is a practical submission advantage, not just a compliance exercise.

Regulatory Status of GMLP

GMLP is guidance, not regulation — it does not have binding legal force. However, FDA's 2021 AI/ML Action Plan explicitly commits to using GMLP as a framework for AI/ML SaMD oversight. MDCG 2025-6 references GMLP as a relevant development framework for EU AI Act Article 9 and 10 compliance. Health Canada and MHRA have both committed to GMLP in their regulatory frameworks. In practice, GMLP compliance is the expected baseline for any AI/ML device submission to any of these regulators.

// 02 — The 10 Principles

All 10 GMLP Principles with Practical Implementation

Principle 01
Multi-Disciplinary Expertise
Teams developing, deploying, and evaluating AI/ML-enabled devices include multidisciplinary expertise — including clinical domain experts, data scientists, regulatory professionals, and intended users.
Principle 02
Good Software Engineering Practices
Established software development practices — documentation, version control, testing, IEC 62304 lifecycle — are implemented. The software engineering discipline underpins AI/ML reliability.
Principle 03
Clinical Study Design Is Relevant
Participants and data are representative of the intended patient population. The "clinical study" framework applies to AI validation — endpoint pre-specification, population definition, sample size justification.
Principle 04
Training Data Independence
Training, tuning, and test datasets are rigorously separated to provide an unbiased evaluation. No patient data flows from training into test. Cross-site independence enforced.
Principle 05
Selected Reference Datasets
Reference datasets are selected to be representative of the clinical population and reflect real-world variability — including equipment variability, site variability, and demographic diversity.
Principle 06
Manage Dataset & Algorithm Bias
Risks of bias are examined and mitigated. Subgroup performance is analysed. Residual biases are transparently documented and addressed in labelling. (Module 04 covers this in full.)
Principle 07
Model Development Is Iterative and Transparent
Model design, training, and performance evaluation is transparent and repeatable. Training code, hyperparameters, and architecture decisions are documented and version-controlled. Results are reproducible.
Principle 08
Interpretability Informs Confidence
Relevant clinical and technical performance of AI/ML models is communicated with context-appropriate clarity — including uncertainty quantification (confidence scores, confidence intervals) and explainability tools (SHAP, Grad-CAM) where appropriate for clinical use.
Principle 09
Evaluation Sets Are Reflective of Clinical Use
Testing reflects the clinical conditions of deployment — including real-world edge cases, data quality variation, and the human-in-the-loop clinical workflow. Controlled laboratory performance alone is insufficient.
Principle 10
Monitoring of Deployed Models
Performance of deployed AI/ML models is monitored in real-world use. Mechanisms exist to detect performance drift, population shift, and emerging safety signals. Actions are defined for detected performance changes. (Module 06 covers this in full.)

// 04 — Summary

Key Takeaways

GMLP Is the Universal AI/ML Baseline

FDA, Health Canada, MHRA, and (via MDCG 2025-6) EU regulators all reference GMLP. Structure your submission documentation around the 10 principles — it creates a instantly recognisable, regulator-friendly structure that crosses jurisdictions.

Principle 10 Extends Beyond Submission

GMLP Principle 10 — monitoring of deployed models — is a post-market obligation that begins on day one of commercial deployment and continues for the device's entire commercial life. Budget for post-market performance monitoring infrastructure as a cost of doing business in AI medical devices.

Multi-Disciplinary Team Is Non-Negotiable

GMLP Principle 1 is not aspirational — regulators assess whether your development team actually includes clinical expertise. A pure data science team without clinical domain experts, RA professionals, and human factors expertise will produce a submission with gaps that a multi-disciplinary team would have caught.

Transparency Reduces Review Risk

GMLP Principle 7 — transparent development — means documenting your model selection process, including models that didn't work and why. A submission that reads like the developer chose the first model they tried is less credible than one that documents an iterative, transparent development process.

Next Step Recommendation

Print the GMLP principles and use them as a development checklist from your first sprint. At each project milestone, ask: "Which GMLP principles are we evidencing right now?" Build GMLP compliance into your development workflow — not as a retroactive documentation exercise.
