// 01 — Foundation

What GMLP Is and Why It Exists

Good Machine Learning Practice (GMLP) is a set of 10 guiding principles developed jointly by FDA, Health Canada, and the UK MHRA, published in October 2021. It represents the first international regulatory consensus on what constitutes responsible AI/ML medical device development — analogous to GCP (Good Clinical Practice) for clinical trials or GMP (Good Manufacturing Practice) for device manufacturing.

GMLP does not specify technical implementation — it specifies principles that development programmes must embody. Regulators evaluate whether a manufacturer's processes, documentation, and validation evidence demonstrate adherence to these principles. A submission that clearly maps to GMLP principles is structured in a way FDA, Health Canada, and MHRA reviewers find immediately legible — this is a practical submission advantage, not just a compliance exercise.

Regulatory Status of GMLP

GMLP is guidance, not regulation — it does not have binding legal force. However, FDA's 2021 AI/ML Action Plan explicitly commits to using GMLP as a framework for AI/ML SaMD oversight. MDCG 2025-6 references GMLP as a relevant development framework for EU AI Act Article 9 and 10 compliance. Health Canada and MHRA have both committed to GMLP in their regulatory frameworks. In practice, GMLP compliance is the expected baseline for any AI/ML device submission to any of these regulators.

// 02 — The 10 Principles

All 10 GMLP Principles with Practical Implementation

Principle 01
Multi-Disciplinary Expertise
Teams developing, deploying, and evaluating AI/ML-enabled devices must include diverse expertise: clinical domain experts who understand the intended use population and clinical workflow; data scientists with ML engineering competency; regulatory affairs professionals who translate technical evidence into submission language; software engineers implementing IEC 62304 practices; and human factors specialists who evaluate how clinicians will interact with and act on AI outputs. In practice, this means the PCCP, clinical validation protocol, and bias analysis should all bear sign-off from multiple disciplines — not just the data science team.
⬡ Real-World Example

Arterys (cardiac MRI AI): When developing their FDA-cleared cardiac segmentation tool, Arterys assembled a team of cardiologists, radiologists, ML engineers, and RA specialists. FDA reviewers specifically noted in the De Novo decision that the team's clinical depth was evident in how the validation protocol was designed — including the choice of clinical endpoints and subgroup definitions. Teams that lack clinical expertise typically propose technically correct but clinically irrelevant performance metrics.

Principle 02
Good Software Engineering Practices
AI/ML development must be grounded in the same rigorous software engineering discipline required for any Class II/III SaMD: full IEC 62304 lifecycle documentation (software development plan, architecture documentation, unit/integration/system testing records, SOUP management); version control with tagged releases for every model trained; code review processes; and automated regression testing. The IEC 62304 safety class (A, B, or C) determines the depth of documentation required. For most diagnostic AI, Class C applies — meaning full unit-level design specifications and traceability from requirements to test results are mandatory.
⬡ Real-World Example

IDx-DR (diabetic retinopathy screening): The first FDA-authorised autonomous AI diagnostic device (De Novo 2018) had its software lifecycle documented at IEC 62304 Class C. IDx published their development methodology, showing full traceability from clinical requirements (sensitivity ≥85% for more-than-mild DR) through algorithm design to V&V test results. This traceability — a software engineering practice, not an AI-specific one — was directly cited by FDA in their De Novo decision as evidence of a controlled development process.

Principle 03
Clinical Study Design Is Relevant
AI validation must be treated with the same methodological rigour as a clinical study: pre-specified primary and secondary endpoints before any model training begins; a formally defined study population matching the intended use population; sample size justification with statistical power calculations; prospective data collection plans or retrospective data selection criteria documented before data access; and an analysis plan covering both primary performance metrics and subgroup analyses. The moment you allow the model's preliminary performance to influence endpoint or subgroup selection, you have introduced bias that regulators will detect and challenge.
⬡ Real-World Example

Viz.ai LVO stroke detection (510k K192061): Viz.ai's AI for large vessel occlusion detection was validated using a pre-specified clinical study protocol registered before data lock. The protocol defined sensitivity and specificity thresholds, the reference standard (neuroradiologist consensus read), and subgroup analyses by stroke severity and time-to-scan. FDA's 510(k) summary explicitly references the pre-specified nature of the performance thresholds — a direct GMLP P3 compliance signal that accelerated review.

Principle 04
Training Data Independence
The test set — used for final performance reporting in the regulatory submission — must be completely independent from training data at the patient level, not just the scan level. If Patient A has multiple scans, all of Patient A's scans must be in the same partition (training OR test, never split across both). Cross-site independence is equally critical: test data from sites that contributed training data will systematically overestimate real-world performance because site-specific acquisition characteristics are embedded in the training. The test set must be locked before final model training, and evaluated exactly once — not iteratively adjusted based on results.
⬡ Real-World Example

Google DeepMind diabetic retinopathy (Gulshan et al., JAMA 2016): The validation study used a completely independent EyePACS-1 test set (n=9,963) and Messidor-2 set (n=1,748) from institutions that contributed no training data. When a follow-up real-world study at Moorfields Eye Hospital was conducted, performance dropped modestly — illustrating that even well-designed test sets may not fully capture deployment variability. This outcome directly motivated GMLP P4's emphasis on true site-level independence.

Principle 05
Selected Reference Datasets
Representativeness must be evaluated and documented across five dimensions: (1) Demographics — age, sex, race/ethnicity distributions matching the intended use population; (2) Disease spectrum — full spectrum of disease severity including edge cases and disease mimics; (3) Geographic and site diversity — multiple institutions, ideally in multiple countries if global clearance is sought; (4) Acquisition equipment diversity — multiple scanner manufacturers, acquisition protocols, and image quality levels; (5) Temporal diversity — data spanning multiple years to capture practice evolution. Regulators expect a formal representativeness statement — not just a description of what data was collected, but evidence that the distribution matches the deployment context.
⬡ Real-World Example

Sepsis AI alerts (Epic Deterioration Index): A landmark study published in JAMA (2021) evaluated Epic's AI sepsis alert across multiple hospitals and found substantially different performance across sites and patient demographics. The root cause: the training dataset, while large, was not representative of all patient populations and clinical workflows where the model was deployed. This case is now widely cited in regulatory guidance development as the defining example of why GMLP P5 representativeness requirements are non-negotiable.

Principle 06
Manage Dataset and Algorithm Bias
Bias analysis is not optional — it is an explicit requirement in FDA submissions (AI/ML Action Plan), EU AI Act Article 10, and MDCG 2025-6. The analysis must cover: identification of potential bias sources (underrepresented demographics, non-uniform labelling practices, equipment-specific signal characteristics); measurement of subgroup performance with confidence intervals; root-cause analysis of observed disparities; and mitigation strategies with documented outcomes. If bias cannot be fully mitigated, it must be disclosed in the IFU as a labelling contraindication or limitation. The submission should include a formal bias analysis section — not just a note in the dataset description.
⬡ Real-World Example

Dermatology AI (Esteva et al., Nature 2017 / follow-up bias studies): The Stanford dermatology CNN achieved dermatologist-level performance in the original validation — but subsequent analyses showed significantly lower sensitivity for skin conditions on darker skin tones, because training datasets (ISIC archive) were predominantly composed of lighter-skinned patient images. The FDA's increasing specificity about subgroup reporting in AI dermatology submissions directly traces to this case. Manufacturers now routinely include Fitzpatrick skin tone as a stratification variable in validation studies.

Principle 07
Model Development Is Iterative and Transparent
Regulatory submissions must document the development process, not just the final model. This means: a model selection rationale (why this architecture over alternatives considered); hyperparameter search documentation (what was tuned, what ranges were explored, what optimisation strategy was used); training convergence records; and documentation of models that were evaluated and rejected. Transparency about the development process is what distinguishes a credible regulatory submission from one that presents only the best result without context. FDA reviewers are increasingly asking "how many models were trained?" — if the answer was hundreds and only the best is reported, the test set performance is likely inflated.
⬡ Real-World Example

Caption Health echo AI (510k K201704): Caption Health's ultrasound image quality guidance AI submission documented their iterative model development explicitly — including comparison of CNN architectures, training data augmentation strategies explored, and the rationale for the final architecture selection. FDA's clearance decision noted the transparency of the development documentation as a positive factor. Compare this to submissions that present a single model with no development history — FDA reviewers describe these as "appearing to have cherry-picked the best result."

Principle 08
Interpretability Informs Confidence
AI outputs presented to clinicians must be accompanied by interpretability information that enables appropriate clinical use. This has two components: quantitative uncertainty — the model should output a confidence score or probability estimate, not just a binary prediction, and the IFU must explain how to interpret these scores clinically; and explainability — for high-risk clinical decisions, visual explainability tools (Grad-CAM heatmaps for imaging AI, SHAP values for tabular data AI) help clinicians identify when the AI is attending to clinically irrelevant features. EU AI Act Article 13 makes this a hard requirement for high-risk AI — users must be able to "interpret the system's output and use it appropriately."
⬡ Real-World Example

Aidoc brain bleed detection: Aidoc's intracranial haemorrhage AI displays a confidence score alongside its alert flag, and marks the region of interest on the CT image using a saliency overlay. This design directly implements GMLP P8 — radiologists can see not just the alert, but which image region triggered it and with what confidence. When errors occur (false positives), the overlay allows radiologists to immediately identify that the AI attended to an artefact rather than pathology, and dismiss the alert with clinical justification. This design pattern is now considered best practice for diagnostic imaging AI.

Principle 09
Evaluation Sets Reflective of Clinical Use
Standalone technical performance (sensitivity and specificity on a curated dataset) is necessary but not sufficient. The evaluation must also include: clinical use simulation — testing in a workflow that mirrors actual deployment, including realistic image quality, realistic case mix, and realistic clinical time pressure; human-AI team evaluation — measuring performance of clinicians WITH the AI vs. WITHOUT, not just standalone AI performance; and failure mode analysis — systematic evaluation of cases where the model fails and characterisation of the failure pattern (e.g., fails on specific equipment types, specific disease presentations). FDA increasingly requests reader studies for high-risk diagnostic AI as a condition of clearance.
⬡ Real-World Example

iRhythm Zio AF detection (510k K192104): iRhythm's atrial fibrillation detection AI was evaluated not just for standalone algorithm sensitivity/specificity, but in a reader study comparing cardiologist performance with and without the AI flag. The reader study showed that cardiologist+AI performance exceeded cardiologist-alone performance on AF detection from 14-day Holter data. FDA's clearance letter specifically cited the reader study design — individual algorithm performance was reported separately but the clinical use evaluation was the human-AI team result that supported the intended use claim.

Principle 10
Monitoring of Deployed Models
Post-market performance monitoring for AI/ML SaMD must be planned before deployment, not retrofitted after launch. The monitoring plan must specify: performance metrics to be tracked continuously (sensitivity, specificity, positive predictive value at minimum); drift detection thresholds — pre-specified performance boundaries that, if breached, trigger investigation; ground truth collection methodology — how real-world labels will be obtained (outcome linkage, clinician override tracking, audit sampling); subgroup monitoring — performance tracked by the same demographic and clinical subgroups evaluated pre-market; and escalation procedures — what happens when a threshold breach is detected (investigation, temporary suspension, Field Safety Corrective Action). GMLP P10 is implemented in detail in Module 06.
⬡ Real-World Example

Paige.AI prostate cancer detection: Following FDA De Novo clearance (2021), Paige implemented a real-world performance monitoring programme tracking sensitivity and specificity across participating pathology labs on a rolling quarterly basis. When performance on samples from one lab network showed a statistically significant drop in sensitivity, investigation revealed a change in tissue processing protocol at that network — not an algorithm failure. This outcome illustrates both why GMLP P10 monitoring is essential (the performance drop was real and patient-safety-relevant) and how it enables root-cause differentiation (algorithm drift vs. data distribution shift).

// 04 — Summary

Key Takeaways

GMLP Is the Universal AI/ML Baseline

FDA, Health Canada, MHRA, and (via MDCG 2025-6) EU regulators all reference GMLP. Structure your submission documentation around the 10 principles — it creates a instantly recognisable, regulator-friendly structure that crosses jurisdictions.

Principle 10 Extends Beyond Submission

GMLP Principle 10 — monitoring of deployed models — is a post-market obligation that begins on day one of commercial deployment and continues for the device's entire commercial life. Budget for post-market performance monitoring infrastructure as a cost of doing business in AI medical devices.

Multi-Disciplinary Team Is Non-Negotiable

GMLP Principle 1 is not aspirational — regulators assess whether your development team actually includes clinical expertise. A pure data science team without clinical domain experts, RA professionals, and human factors expertise will produce a submission with gaps that a multi-disciplinary team would have caught.

Transparency Reduces Review Risk

GMLP Principle 7 — transparent development — means documenting your model selection process, including models that didn't work and why. A submission that reads like the developer chose the first model they tried is less credible than one that documents an iterative, transparent development process.

Next Step Recommendation

Print the GMLP principles and use them as a development checklist from your first sprint. At each project milestone, ask: "Which GMLP principles are we evidencing right now?" Build GMLP compliance into your development workflow — not as a retroactive documentation exercise.
