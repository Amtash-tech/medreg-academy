// 01 — Foundation

Why AI Devices Require Specialised Post-Market Monitoring

Post-market surveillance for traditional software medical devices primarily monitors for use errors, software defects, and adverse events. For AI/ML devices, an additional critical failure mode exists: performance drift. A locked AI algorithm that performed at 94% sensitivity at clearance may perform at 78% sensitivity in real-world deployment — due to differences between the validation population and the deployed population, equipment variation, workflow integration, or temporal shift in disease patterns.

Performance drift can occur without any software change, without any hardware failure, and without any user error. The algorithm has not "broken" — the world has moved. Detecting this requires proactive, quantitative performance monitoring that goes well beyond traditional medical device PMS. GMLP Principle 10, FDA's AI/ML Action Plan, and EU MDR PMCF requirements all mandate this monitoring.

Regulatory Obligations

FDA AI/ML Action Plan (2021): real-world performance monitoring framework for AI/ML SaMD is a core pillar. EU MDR Annex XIV Part B: PMCF is mandatory for all device classes — for AI devices, PMCF must specifically address ongoing algorithm performance. GMLP Principle 10: monitoring of deployed models required. EU AI Act Article 72: post-market monitoring system mandatory for all high-risk AI systems. The AI Act adds 15-day serious incident reporting to EASA on top of MDR's existing obligations.

// 03 — Monitoring Architecture

Building a Post-Market AI Monitoring System

1

Define Performance Metrics and Thresholds

Before deployment, pre-specify the performance metrics you will monitor (sensitivity, specificity, AUC, calibration, subgroup disparities) and the threshold values that will trigger an investigation. These thresholds should be clinically meaningful — not arbitrary statistical significance levels. A 2% absolute drop in sensitivity may be clinically significant for a high-stakes diagnostic; a 5% drop may be acceptable for a low-stakes screening application. Document the clinical rationale for each threshold in your PMS plan, reviewed and approved by clinical advisors.

2

Establish Ground Truth Collection

Real-world performance monitoring requires real-world ground truth — the actual clinical outcomes for cases processed by your AI. This is the hardest operational challenge in post-market AI monitoring. Options: (1) Outcome linkage — link AI outputs to downstream clinical outcomes (pathology results, follow-up imaging, clinical outcomes) in your customer's electronic health record. (2) Clinical audit sampling — periodic expert review of a random sample of AI outputs against expert re-read. (3) Implicit feedback — monitoring AI override rates (when clinicians override AI recommendations, this signals potential performance degradation). Each approach has limitations; a combination is most robust.

3

Implement Statistical Process Control

Use statistical process control (SPC) methods to distinguish true performance drift from random variation. CUSUM (Cumulative Sum) charts detect sustained performance shifts. EWMA (Exponentially Weighted Moving Average) charts are sensitive to gradual trends. Define your control limits pre-deployment. When a metric crosses a control limit, it triggers investigation — not an automatic response, but a structured root cause analysis to determine whether the signal represents true drift or statistical noise.

4

Define Investigation and Response Procedures

For each tier of performance concern, define a pre-specified response: Watch (within control limits but trending toward boundary — increase monitoring frequency). Investigate (control limit crossed — root cause analysis initiated, clinical advisory board notified). Act (investigation confirms significant performance degradation — response options: customer advisory, PCCP-covered retraining, new regulatory submission, or device field correction). Document these tiers and response procedures in your PMS plan before deployment.

5

Subgroup Monitoring

Monitor performance separately for each pre-specified demographic and clinical subgroup — the same subgroups analysed in your pre-market bias analysis (Module 04). Performance drift can be subgroup-specific: an algorithm may maintain overall performance while degrading substantially in elderly patients or patients with comorbidities. Aggregate monitoring alone will miss this. Build subgroup-stratified monitoring dashboards and set separate alert thresholds for each subgroup.

// 04 — Regulatory Reporting

Reporting Obligations for AI Performance Events

FDA Medical Device Reporting (MDR)

Under 21 CFR Part 803, AI performance degradation that contributes to or could contribute to serious injury or death is a reportable adverse event (30-day MDR) or malfunction that would be reportable if it recurred (5-day MDR for immediately life-threatening). Performance drift that results in demonstrable patient harm — missed diagnoses, delayed treatment — must be assessed for MDR reporting obligation. Systematic underperformance in a subgroup may qualify as a reportable malfunction.

FDA Field Corrections and Removals

Under 21 CFR Part 806, if you take action (software update, algorithm retrain, customer advisory) to address a performance issue that could cause or contribute to serious injury or death, you must report this correction within 10 working days. PCCP-covered retraining that is triggered by detected performance drift may constitute a reportable correction if the drift presented patient risk.

EU MDR Vigilance Reporting

Under EU MDR Article 87, serious incidents (death or serious health deterioration) and field safety corrective actions must be reported to national competent authorities. For AI devices: if post-market monitoring identifies systematic performance degradation causing patient harm, this triggers vigilance reporting. Timeline: serious incidents within 2–15 days (severity-dependent); field safety corrective actions within 10 days of decision to take action.

EU AI Act Serious Incident Reporting

EU AI Act Article 73 requires providers of high-risk AI systems to report serious incidents to the relevant national market surveillance authority within 15 days of becoming aware. A serious incident for AI is: any malfunction or underperformance of an AI system that directly or indirectly leads to death, serious injury, serious damage to property, or serious harm to the environment. This is in addition to, not instead of, MDR vigilance reporting.

// 05 — Summary

Key Takeaways

Drift Happens Without Software Changes

A locked, unchanged algorithm can degrade in performance due to population shift, equipment changes, or evolving disease patterns. Traditional software PMS — which primarily monitors for bugs and use errors — will not detect performance drift. You need quantitative performance monitoring as a dedicated, additional PMS activity.

Ground Truth Is the Hard Problem

You cannot monitor performance without ground truth. Design your ground truth collection mechanism before deployment — not after a performance concern arises. Outcome linkage with hospital EHRs, clinical audit sampling programmes, and override rate monitoring should all be contractually established with your customer sites before commercial launch.

Pre-Specify Thresholds

Define your performance monitoring thresholds and trigger criteria before deployment, in your PMS plan. Deciding thresholds after seeing data creates a conflict of interest — and regulators examining your post-market file will identify post-hoc threshold setting as a process weakness.

Dual Reporting: MDR + AI Act

For EU-marketed AI medical devices, serious performance events trigger both MDR vigilance reporting and EU AI Act Article 73 reporting — to potentially different authorities, on different timelines (15 days AI Act vs 2–15 days MDR). Build both reporting pathways into your pharmacovigilance SOP before launch.
