Case 01
Is This App a Medical Device? A Cardiac Risk Calculator
A software developer builds a mobile app that calculates 10-year cardiovascular risk using the Framingham Risk Score based on user-entered values (age, cholesterol, blood pressure, smoking status). The app displays a percentage risk score and recommends "consult your doctor if score > 20%." The developer believes this is a wellness app and not subject to FDA regulation. Are they correct?
SaMD DefinitionIntended UseCDS Exemption

🔍 Key Facts

Product: Mobile app calculating cardiovascular risk score from user-entered clinical values

Output: 10-year CVD risk percentage with a threshold-based recommendation to consult a physician

Claim: Developer believes it is a wellness app, not a regulated medical device

Question: Does this software meet the definition of a medical device under 21 USC 321(h) and IMDRF N10?

⚖️ Regulatory Framework Applied

Step 1 — Device definition test: Is it intended to diagnose, cure, mitigate, treat, or prevent disease? The Framingham Risk Score is a validated clinical tool for predicting cardiovascular disease risk — a disease-related purpose. This is not general wellness.

Step 2 — CDS exemption test: Under FDA's 2022 CDS Guidance, the app must meet ALL four criteria to be exempt. Does it display the basis for its recommendation so a clinician can independently review? Does a clinician act as intermediary?

📋 Regulatory Analysis — Step by Step

1

Medical purpose confirmed: The Framingham Risk Score is explicitly intended to assess cardiovascular disease risk. The 21 USC 321(h) device definition is met — this software has a disease-related intended purpose.

2

IMDRF N10 SaMD definition met: The app is "software intended to be used for one or more medical purposes that perform these purposes without being part of a hardware medical device." It is SaMD by definition.

3

CDS exemption analysis: The app does NOT meet all four CDS criteria. It provides a risk score for cardiovascular disease — a serious condition. FDA's CDS Guidance states that CDS for serious or critical conditions does not qualify for the non-device CDS exemption regardless of whether it displays its basis.

4

Classification: This SaMD likely falls in IMDRF N12 Category II (informs clinical management for a serious condition). Under FDA, it would likely be Class II, requiring a 510(k) or potentially qualifying as a De Novo if no predicate exists.

5

Developer's assumption is incorrect: This is a regulated medical device. The developer must register, list the device, and seek 510(k) clearance or De Novo authorization before marketing in the US.

// Correct Determination
The app IS a medical device. The developer must obtain FDA clearance (most likely 510(k)) before marketing. The CDS exemption does not apply because cardiovascular disease is a serious condition. Continuing to market without clearance constitutes unlawful distribution of an uncleared device under 21 USC 331.

Case 02
EU MDR Rule 11 Classification: ICU Deterioration Alert Software
A hospital software company develops a cloud-based SaMD that continuously analyzes vital sign streams from ICU patients and generates real-time alerts when the patient's condition suggests deterioration. The alert is reviewed by a nurse who then decides whether to escalate to a physician. The manufacturer self-certified the software as Class I under EU MDR, arguing that the nurse makes the final decision. Is this classification correct?
EU MDR Rule 11MDSW QualificationClass IIb

🔍 Key Facts

Product: Cloud SaMD analyzing ICU vital sign streams, generating deterioration alerts

User: Nurse reviews alert and decides whether to escalate

Claimed class: Class I (self-certified by manufacturer)

Justification given: Human clinician makes the final action decision, not the software

⚖️ Regulatory Framework Applied

MDCG 2021-24 Step 1 — MDSW Qualification: Does the software have a medical purpose? Yes — patient monitoring for clinical deterioration. Does it create or modify medical information rather than just store/display? Yes — it analyzes data and generates alerts. MDSW confirmed.

EU MDR Rule 11 — Classification: The classification depends on the intended purpose and severity of potential harm from incorrect information, not on whether a human reviews the output.

📋 Regulatory Analysis — Step by Step

1

MDSW qualification confirmed: The software has a medical intended purpose (monitoring critically ill patients for deterioration) and creates new medical information (deterioration alerts) rather than merely displaying raw data. It qualifies as MDSW under MDCG 2021-24.

2

Rule 11 severity analysis: What is the clinical state? ICU patients are in critical or serious condition. What is the potential harm of a missed or false alert in an ICU? A missed deterioration alert in an ICU patient could result in death or irreversible harm. This is clearly a serious healthcare situation.

3

Human intermediary does NOT reduce classification: EU MDR Rule 11 classification is based on the intended purpose and potential consequences of incorrect information — not whether a clinician is in the loop. The MDCG guidance explicitly states that the involvement of a healthcare professional in reviewing output does not lower the risk class.

4

Correct classification — Class IIb: Software providing information used to make decisions with serious health consequences qualifies as Class IIb under Rule 11. ICU deterioration alerts clearly meet this threshold. The manufacturer's Class I self-certification is incorrect.

5

Consequence: The manufacturer must engage a Notified Body for conformity assessment, prepare a full Technical Documentation package per Annex II, conduct a Clinical Evaluation, and implement a PMS system before CE marking.

// Correct Classification: Class IIb
The software is Class IIb under EU MDR Rule 11, not Class I. The manufacturer's self-certification is invalid. A Notified Body must assess conformity. The manufacturer must retract the CE marking, engage an NB, and complete full conformity assessment before legally marketing the device in the EU.

Case 03
510(k) Refused to File: Missing SBOM and Cybersecurity Documentation
A manufacturer submits a 510(k) for a cloud-connected SaMD that integrates with hospital EHR systems for diagnostic imaging analysis. FDA issues a Refuse to File (RTF) letter citing deficiencies in cybersecurity documentation. The manufacturer included only a one-page "cybersecurity summary" describing their password policy and encryption. They are surprised, believing cybersecurity is an IT issue handled by the hospital, not them. What went wrong and what must be corrected?
SBOMFDA RTFPremarket CyberSection 524B

🔍 Key Facts

Product: Cloud-connected SaMD, EHR integration, diagnostic imaging analysis

Submission: 510(k) premarket notification

Cybersecurity provided: One-page summary covering password policy and encryption only

FDA action: Refuse to File (RTF) — submission not accepted for substantive review

Manufacturer belief: Hospital IT is responsible for cybersecurity, not the device manufacturer

⚖️ Regulatory Framework Applied

Section 524B FD&C Act (2023): Manufacturers of "cyber devices" — devices with software that connect to the internet — must submit cybersecurity information. FDA may refuse to file submissions that lack required cybersecurity documentation. This is now statutory, not just guidance.

FDA Cybersecurity Guidance 2023: Specifies six required documentation elements. A one-page summary does not satisfy any of them. The "hospital is responsible" belief is legally incorrect.

📋 What Was Missing and What Is Required

1

SBOM not provided: A machine-readable Software Bill of Materials listing all software components, libraries, versions, and dependencies is required. Without it, FDA cannot assess the vulnerability profile. The SBOM must use a standard format (SPDX or CycloneDX).

2

Threat model absent: A structured threat analysis (STRIDE or equivalent) identifying assets, threat actors, attack surfaces, and mitigations is required. Password policy is a control, not a threat model.

3

Security architecture not documented: Data flow diagrams showing all network interfaces, authentication mechanisms, encryption implementation, and trust boundaries must be provided.

4

Security testing evidence missing: Results from SAST, DAST, and penetration testing must be provided with documented findings and resolutions.

5

No cybersecurity management plan: A plan detailing post-market vulnerability monitoring, patch timelines (30 days critical, 60 days high), and coordinated vulnerability disclosure policy must be submitted.

6

Manufacturer responsibility is non-delegable: Under Section 524B, the device manufacturer — not the hospital — bears statutory responsibility for cybersecurity of their device. The hospital manages its network; the manufacturer is responsible for the device's own security posture.

// What Must Happen Next
The manufacturer must resubmit with a complete cybersecurity package: machine-readable SBOM, threat model, security architecture diagrams, penetration test results, cybersecurity management plan, and vulnerability disclosure policy. They should conduct a full cybersecurity engineering review per FDA 2023 guidance before resubmitting to avoid a second RTF.

Case 04
Critical CVE Discovered in Marketed Device — What Are the Obligations?
A manufacturer has a FDA-cleared infusion pump controller SaMD that has been on the market for two years. A security researcher discloses a critical vulnerability (CVSS 9.4) in an open-source networking library listed in their SBOM. The vulnerability would allow a network-adjacent attacker to execute arbitrary code. The device is deployed in over 2,000 hospitals in the US and EU. The manufacturer's engineering team estimates a patch will take 90 days to develop and test. What must the manufacturer do, in what timeframe, and under which regulations?
CVE ResponsePatch TimelinesNIS2Postmarket Cyber

🔍 Key Facts

Device: FDA-cleared infusion pump controller SaMD, marketed 2 years

Vulnerability: CVSS 9.4 critical in open-source networking library (in SBOM)

Threat: Network-adjacent arbitrary code execution — direct patient safety risk

Scale: 2,000+ hospitals in US and EU

Patch timeline: 90 days estimated by engineering

⚖️ Regulatory Framework Applied

FDA Postmarket Cybersecurity Guidance: Critical vulnerabilities (CVSS ≥9.0) require patch deployment within 30 days. 90 days exceeds this — requires documented justification and interim mitigations.

NIS2 Directive (EU): If hospital customers qualify as essential entities, significant cyber incidents must be reported within 24 hours (early warning) and 72 hours (full notification) to national authorities.

FDA MDR Reporting: If the vulnerability creates a reasonable probability of serious patient harm, MDR reporting obligations may be triggered under 21 CFR Part 803.

📋 Required Actions by Timeframe

24h

Internal escalation and risk assessment: Convene the cybersecurity incident response team. Assess exploitability in the device's typical network deployment. What patient harm could result from arbitrary code execution on this device? Document the risk determination thoroughly.

72h

EU NIS2 reporting: If EU hospital customers qualify as essential entities and this constitutes a significant incident, notify relevant national competent authorities — early warning within 24 hours, full report within 72 hours with impact assessment and initial mitigations.

7d

Customer communication and interim mitigations: Notify affected hospitals of the vulnerability and provide specific compensating controls — network isolation recommendations, firewall rules restricting device network access, enhanced monitoring protocols. Document all customer communications.

30d

FDA 30-day critical patch deadline: FDA expects critical CVE patches within 30 days. Since 90 days is required, the manufacturer must document justification, demonstrate interim compensating controls adequately reduce risk, and proactively communicate this assessment to FDA's CDRH cybersecurity team.

90d

Patch release and deployment: Release validated patch with full IEC 62304 testing documentation. Deploy to all affected devices. Track deployment rates — unpatched devices remain at elevated risk and require continued interim mitigations until patched.

// Key Regulatory Risk
The 90-day patch timeline exceeds FDA's 30-day critical CVE expectation. Without documented justification and demonstrably effective interim compensating controls, this creates significant regulatory exposure. If the vulnerability is actively exploited and patient harm results during the 90-day window, MDR reporting obligations and potential recall liability both apply. Proactive engagement with FDA is strongly recommended rather than waiting for FDA to identify the gap.

Case 05
AI Retinal Screening App — Dataset Bias Identified Post-Launch
A company has FDA-cleared AI software that screens retinal images for diabetic retinopathy. Six months post-launch, a hospital network reports that the software's sensitivity is significantly lower in patients of South Asian descent compared to the overall performance metrics in the 510(k). The training dataset was predominantly composed of images from North American and European clinical sites. What are the manufacturer's obligations, and what should have been done pre-market?
Bias AnalysisGMLPPost-Market Monitoring510(k) Supplement

🔍 Key Facts

Device: FDA-cleared AI retinal screening SaMD for diabetic retinopathy

Issue: Significantly lower sensitivity for South Asian patients discovered post-launch

Root cause: Training data predominantly North American and European — South Asian patients underrepresented

GMLP principles implicated: Principle 5 (representative datasets) and Principle 10 (post-market monitoring)

⚖️ Regulatory Framework Applied

GMLP Principles 4 & 5: Training datasets must be representative of the intended use population including relevant subgroups. Diabetic retinopathy affects South Asian populations at high rates — underrepresentation is a foreseeable and preventable gap.

FDA Pre-Market Expectation: Subgroup performance analysis is expected in AI/ML submissions. Performance gaps should be either mitigated or disclosed as known limitations in device labeling.

📋 Pre-Market Failures and Post-Market Obligations

Pre-1

Dataset composition analysis should have been performed: The manufacturer should have analyzed the demographic composition of training data and identified underrepresentation of South Asian patients before submission. GMLP Principle 5 requires this analysis explicitly.

Pre-2

Subgroup performance reporting should have been included: The 510(k) submission should have included sensitivity/specificity broken down by demographic subgroups. FDA's AI/ML guidance expects subgroup metrics for foreseeable user populations.

Pre-3

IFU limitation disclosure should have been made: If the gap existed and could not be mitigated, it should have been disclosed in labeling: "Performance in South Asian populations has not been fully validated; clinical judgment should not rely solely on this system for this population."

Post-1

Assess patient harm immediately: Determine whether missed diagnoses occurred in South Asian patients during deployment. If patients received incorrect screening results, MDR adverse event reporting may be triggered under 21 CFR Part 803.

Post-2

FDA notification and 510(k) supplement: The performance gap constitutes new safety information. A 510(k) supplement is likely required to update labeling with the known limitation, or to submit the retrained model if bias is corrected.

Post-3

Corrective action — dataset expansion: Collect retinal images from South Asian clinical sites. Retrain and revalidate. If within an approved PCCP scope, deploy without new submission; if not, a supplement is required.

// Regulatory Determination
This case illustrates a pre-market bias analysis failure with serious post-market consequences. The manufacturer faces potential adverse event reporting obligations, a 510(k) supplement for labeling updates, and corrective action. Under EU AI Act Article 10, this bias would have been explicitly required to be identified and addressed before CE marking — demonstrating the added value of the AI Act's data governance requirements for patient safety.

Case 06
Dual Compliance Challenge: AI Pathology SaMD Under MDR and EU AI Act
A digital pathology company has a CE-marked Class IIb AI system that analyzes histopathology slides for colorectal cancer detection. Following the EU AI Act entering into force, their regulatory counsel confirms the system qualifies as high-risk AI under Annex III. The product team believes their existing CE marking automatically satisfies the AI Act. Are they correct, and what additional work is required under MDCG 2025-6?
Dual ComplianceMDCG 2025-6AI Act Annex IIIData Governance

🔍 Key Facts

Device: AI histopathology SaMD for colorectal cancer — CE-marked Class IIb

New obligation: High-risk AI under EU AI Act Annex III

Belief to test: CE marking under MDR automatically satisfies EU AI Act

⚖️ Regulatory Framework Applied

MDCG 2025-6: Explicitly states EU MDR compliance does NOT automatically satisfy EU AI Act. The AI Act adds cumulative, additive obligations.

EU AI Act Art. 9–15, 72: Risk management, data governance, transparency, human oversight, robustness, cybersecurity, post-market monitoring — several go beyond MDR requirements.

📋 Compliance Gaps: MDR vs MDR + AI Act

Gap 1

Data Governance (Art. 10): MDR requires clinical evidence for intended use. AI Act requires formal documentation of training data examination for biases, data management practices for the full data lifecycle, and data quality governance. This documentation must be created and added to the Technical File.

Gap 2

Transparency (Art. 13): MDR IFU covers intended use and instructions. AI Act Art. 13 requires the IFU to enable pathologists to interpret AI outputs appropriately — including the system's scope, limitations, performance on relevant subpopulations, and what it cannot do. The MDR IFU almost certainly needs supplementing.

Gap 3

Human Oversight (Art. 14): MDR does not mandate specific human oversight design provisions. AI Act Art. 14 requires the system to be designed allowing pathologists to monitor, understand, and override AI output. This design and the IFU guidance on exercising oversight must be documented.

Gap 4

AI-Specific Cybersecurity (Art. 15): Beyond MDR GSPR 17.2, the AI Act requires resilience against adversarial attacks on the AI model itself — such as adversarial image perturbations designed to fool the pathology classifier. AI-specific security testing is required.

Gap 5

Post-Market AI Monitoring (Art. 72): MDR requires a PMS system. AI Act additionally requires a system specifically tracking AI performance in real-world deployment — performance drift, subgroup performance over time, unexpected AI behavior patterns.

// Required Actions
The product team is incorrect. MDCG 2025-6 is explicit — CE marking under MDR does not satisfy EU AI Act. The manufacturer must conduct a gap analysis against Articles 9–15 and 72, update Technical Documentation with AI Act-specific sections, revise the IFU, conduct AI-specific security testing, and establish AI-specific post-market monitoring. Notified Body re-engagement may be required depending on the significance of Technical Documentation changes.
MDCG 2025-6EU AI Act Art. 9–15, 72EU AI Act Annex IIIEU MDR 2017/745ISO/IEC 23894

Case 07
De Novo vs 510(k): Novel Sepsis Prediction Algorithm
A startup has developed a novel SaMD that uses machine learning to predict sepsis onset 6 hours before clinical diagnosis by analyzing EHR data streams. Their regulatory team debates whether to pursue a 510(k) with a qSOFA-based sepsis scoring tool as predicate, or a De Novo request. The legal team argues 510(k) is faster and cheaper. The regulatory team is uncertain about substantial equivalence. Who is right and how should the decision be made?
De Novo510(k) PredicateSubstantial EquivalenceNovel Technology

🔍 Key Facts

Device: ML-based SaMD predicting sepsis onset 6 hours in advance from EHR data streams

Proposed predicate: qSOFA-based sepsis scoring software (existing 510(k))

Legal team view: 510(k) — faster and cheaper

Key question: Is substantial equivalence to qSOFA achievable for this ML device?

⚖️ Regulatory Framework Applied

510(k) Substantial Equivalence: Requires same intended use AND either same technological characteristics OR different tech that does not raise new safety/effectiveness questions and performs at least as well.

De Novo: For novel, low-to-moderate risk devices with no valid predicate. Creates a new classification with special controls. Future devices can use the De Novo as a 510(k) predicate.

📋 Predicate Analysis and Pathway Determination

1

Intended use comparison: qSOFA calculates a bedside score from 3 parameters for current sepsis screening. The proposed device predicts sepsis 6 hours in advance using ML on multi-modal EHR streams. The intended uses are similar (sepsis identification) but meaningfully different — prediction vs. screening, 6-hour advance warning vs. current status assessment.

2

Technological characteristics raise new questions: The ML algorithm, EHR multi-modal input, and 6-hour predictive horizon represent fundamentally different technology than qSOFA's simple scoring rules. FDA would likely determine this raises new questions of safety and effectiveness not addressed by the predicate — particularly around AI/ML validation, dataset representativeness, and performance consistency across hospital systems.

3

510(k) NSE risk is significant: If FDA determines Not Substantially Equivalent (NSE), the manufacturer must restart with De Novo or PMA — having lost filing fees and several months. This makes the "faster and cheaper" argument fragile.

4

De Novo is the appropriate pathway: This novel AI/ML sepsis prediction tool with no true predicate is a strong De Novo candidate. It will create a new classification with defined special controls (PCCP for algorithm updates, representative training data requirements, subgroup performance reporting) that benefits future entrants.

5

Pre-Sub meeting is essential: Before committing to either pathway, request a Pre-Submission (Q-Sub) meeting with FDA CDRH. FDA will provide definitive guidance on predicate acceptability and required evidence — avoiding expensive misdirection.

// Recommendation
De Novo is the more appropriate pathway. The legal team's "faster and cheaper" argument underestimates the substantial NSE risk given the technological differences from qSOFA. A failed 510(k) followed by De Novo is slower and more expensive than De Novo from the start. The manufacturer should request a Pre-Sub meeting with FDA to confirm the pathway before committing resources to a full submission package.

Case 08
Locked vs. Adaptive: Do I Need a New 510(k) to Update My AI Model?
A company has a 510(k)-cleared AI dermatology SaMD that classifies skin lesions. The clinical team wants to retrain the model on 50,000 additional images from three new clinical sites to improve sensitivity. Engineering says "it's just improving the model — no new submission needed." The regulatory team is unsure. Who is right, and what process must be followed?
Locked AlgorithmPCCP510(k) SupplementSignificant Change

🔍 Key Facts

Device: 510(k)-cleared AI SaMD for skin lesion classification

Proposed change: Retrain on 50,000 additional images from 3 new clinical sites

Engineering claim: No submission needed — just improving the model

PCCP status: No PCCP was included in the original 510(k)

⚖️ Regulatory Framework Applied

Locked algorithm principle: The cleared 510(k) was for a specific locked version. All performance data submitted to FDA was for that version. Retraining changes model weights and therefore changes the cleared device.

Significant change assessment: FDA guidance requires manufacturers to assess whether changes to cleared devices require new submissions. Algorithm retraining is a substantive modification.

📋 Analysis and Required Process

1

Retraining is a significant change requiring assessment: Retraining on new data changes the model's parameters and real-world behavior. This is not a minor bug fix — it is a substantive modification to the cleared device's core function. Engineering's assumption is incorrect without formal documented assessment.

2

No PCCP means no automatic pathway: Without an approved PCCP, every modification must be individually assessed. There is no pre-approved change control framework covering this retraining.

3

Change control assessment must be documented: Does this change affect intended use? Could it affect performance in a way impacting safety or effectiveness? Retraining on new site data could improve OR inadvertently degrade performance for the original population — unknown until validation is complete.

4

New 510(k) supplement is almost certainly required: A fundamental change to the AI algorithm through retraining on significantly different data from new sites requires a new 510(k) or supplement. The manufacturer cannot determine this without completing validation and comparing performance to the cleared version.

5

Lesson: include PCCP in initial submissions: Had the original 510(k) included a PCCP anticipating "retraining on additional clinical site data," the approved PCCP would allow this change — if performance criteria are met — without a new submission. This is the core value of PCCP planning.

// Correct Process
The regulatory team is correct to be cautious — engineering's assumption is wrong. The manufacturer must complete validation, formally assess whether the change is significant (it almost certainly is for algorithm retraining), and submit a new 510(k) or supplement before deploying the updated model. Deploying an uncleared algorithm modification constitutes distribution of an uncleared device under 21 USC 331.
