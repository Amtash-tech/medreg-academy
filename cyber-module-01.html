// 01 — Legal Foundation

FDA's Statutory Authority: Section 524B

Prior to 2023, FDA's ability to require cybersecurity documentation in premarket submissions rested on general safety and effectiveness authority under the FD&C Act. Manufacturers could, in principle, argue cybersecurity requirements were guidance-only. The Consolidated Appropriations Act of 2023 ended that debate permanently.

Section 524B of the FD&C Act (enacted March 23, 2023) gives FDA explicit statutory authority to require cybersecurity information in premarket submissions for cyber devices — and to refuse to accept submissions that do not include this information. This transforms cybersecurity from a best-practice expectation into a statutory requirement.

What Is a "Cyber Device"? (Section 524B(c))

A device that: (1) includes software validated, installed, or authorized by the manufacturer; AND (2) has the ability to connect to the internet; AND (3) contains technological characteristics that could be vulnerable to cybersecurity threats. This definition captures virtually all modern SaMD, networked medical devices, and connected hardware platforms. The key test is the combination of software + internet connectivity + vulnerability potential — all three must be present.

// 02 — The Six Required Elements

FDA 2023 Premarket Guidance: Six Mandatory Submission Elements

FDA's "Cybersecurity in Medical Devices: Quality System Considerations and Content of Premarket Submissions" (final guidance, September 2023) defines six elements that every cyber device submission must include. Missing any element will result in a Refuse to Accept (RTA) determination:

1

Cybersecurity Management Plan

A documented plan for monitoring, identifying, and addressing cybersecurity vulnerabilities throughout the device lifecycle. Must cover: how the manufacturer will identify vulnerabilities (CVE monitoring sources, threat intelligence feeds), the patch and update process, customer communication procedures for security updates, and the coordinated vulnerability disclosure policy. This is a living document — not a one-time pre-market artefact.

2

Software Bill of Materials (SBOM)

A machine-readable inventory of all software components in the device — including commercial off-the-shelf software, open-source components, and proprietary code. Must be provided in a standard format (SPDX or CycloneDX). Required fields: component name, version, supplier, dependencies, and known vulnerabilities. The SBOM must be maintained and updated when software changes. Module 02 covers SBOM in full detail.

3

Threat Model

A systematic analysis of potential cybersecurity threats to the device. FDA recommends STRIDE (Spoofing, Tampering, Repudiation, Information Disclosure, Denial of Service, Elevation of Privilege) or an equivalent structured methodology. Must cover all device interfaces (physical ports, wireless, network connections, update mechanisms), authentication pathways, data flows, and third-party component attack surfaces. Module 03 covers STRIDE threat modelling in full.

4

Security Architecture Documentation

A description of the device's security design — how security controls are implemented, not just what they are. Must include: network diagrams showing all interfaces and data flows, authentication and authorisation mechanisms, encryption implementation (data in transit and at rest), security boundaries between device subsystems, and how the device handles untrusted inputs. Architecture documentation must be consistent with the threat model findings.

5

Security Testing Evidence

Evidence that the device has been tested for cybersecurity vulnerabilities. FDA expects a layered testing approach: SAST (Static Application Security Testing — automated code analysis), DAST (Dynamic Application Security Testing — runtime analysis), fuzz testing (automated boundary and protocol fuzzing), and penetration testing (manual expert assessment). Test scope must align with the threat model — threats identified must be tested against.

6

Vulnerability Disclosure Policy (VDP)

A publicly available policy describing how the manufacturer will receive, triage, and respond to vulnerability reports from researchers, customers, and the public. Must include: how to report a vulnerability (contact information or portal), acknowledgement timeline, assessment and response timeline, and coordination approach with CISA ICS-CERT and FDA for high-severity vulnerabilities. FDA and CISA expect coordinated disclosure — unilateral disclosure without manufacturer notification is discouraged but must be accommodated.

// 03 — TPLC Approach

Total Product Lifecycle (TPLC) Security

FDA's cybersecurity framework is built on the principle that security cannot be achieved at a single point in time. The device must be secure at launch — and remain secure throughout its marketed lifetime. This is the TPLC (Total Product Lifecycle) security philosophy that underpins all FDA cybersecurity guidance.

Security by Design (Pre-Market)

Threat modelling, security architecture, and security testing must occur during development — not as a post-development audit. Security requirements must be defined alongside functional requirements in the Software Requirements Specification. Risk management under ISO 14971 must explicitly address cybersecurity hazards in addition to functional safety hazards.

Continuous Monitoring (Post-Market)

Manufacturers must actively monitor for new vulnerabilities affecting their deployed device throughout its commercial life. This means: subscribing to CVE feeds (NVD, CISA Known Exploited Vulnerabilities), monitoring component-specific vulnerability databases, tracking SBOM components against new CVE disclosures, and conducting periodic security assessments of fielded devices.

Patch and Update Management

Security patches must be deployable throughout the device lifecycle. FDA expects manufacturers to design devices with updatable software from the outset. Devices that cannot be patched remotely require documented compensating controls. Patch timelines are defined by CVSS severity score (Module 04 covers postmarket timelines).

Coordinated Vulnerability Disclosure

When a vulnerability is discovered or reported, manufacturers must coordinate with FDA and CISA ICS-CERT before public disclosure. FDA expects notification within 30 days of confirming a cybersecurity risk that could cause harm. Uncoordinated disclosure that creates patient risk can trigger FDA enforcement action.

// 04 — Summary

Key Takeaways

Section 524B Is Law, Not Guidance

Cybersecurity submission requirements for cyber devices are statutory under the Consolidated Appropriations Act 2023. FDA can and will issue Refuse to Accept decisions for submissions missing required cybersecurity elements. This is no longer negotiable.

Six Elements, All Mandatory

Cyber Management Plan, SBOM, Threat Model, Security Architecture, Security Testing, and VDP — all six must be present in the submission. Missing any one triggers RTA. Build all six into your development programme from the start, not as a submission-time exercise.

TPLC Means Lifecycle Obligation

FDA clearance is not the end of your cybersecurity obligations — it is the beginning. You must monitor, patch, and disclose throughout the device's commercial life. Design your update infrastructure and vulnerability monitoring programme before you submit.

Align Cybersecurity with ISO 14971

Cybersecurity risks are device risks under ISO 14971. Your risk management file must include cybersecurity threat-derived hazards, risk estimates (using CVSS or equivalent), and risk controls. The threat model and the risk management file must be consistent and cross-referenced.

Next Step Recommendation

Immediately after determining your device is a "cyber device": (1) Initiate threat modelling in parallel with functional requirements — do not wait until design is frozen. (2) Generate your initial SBOM from your dependency management system from the first day of development. (3) Draft your Vulnerability Disclosure Policy and publish it before market entry — it must exist before a vulnerability is reported. Module 02 covers SBOM requirements in comprehensive detail.
