// 01 — Foundation

The Postmarket Cybersecurity Obligation

FDA clearance does not end your cybersecurity obligations — it begins the most demanding phase: maintaining security across a deployed device fleet for the entire commercial lifetime of the product. New vulnerabilities are discovered in software components every day. Your device, once cleared, will face an evolving threat landscape that did not exist at the time of submission.

FDA's postmarket cybersecurity framework is defined in "Postmarket Management of Cybersecurity in Medical Devices" (2016, updated by 2023 guidance). The framework is built on the concept of controlled risk vs uncontrolled risk — a binary determination that governs whether and how quickly you must respond to a discovered vulnerability.

Controlled vs Uncontrolled Risk

Controlled risk: Residual vulnerability risk that is adequately managed through compensating controls (network segmentation, monitoring, operational workarounds) without requiring a device patch. No mandatory reporting to FDA required; document the risk assessment and controls in your postmarket file. Uncontrolled risk: Vulnerability where compensating controls alone are insufficient to manage patient risk to an acceptable level. A device patch is required, and FDA must be notified based on the severity and patient harm potential.

// 02 — Patch Timelines

CVSS-Based Patch Timelines

FDA's postmarket guidance ties vulnerability response timelines to CVSS (Common Vulnerability Scoring System) severity scores. CVSS v3.1 is the current industry standard — it produces a score from 0 to 10 based on exploitability, scope, and impact metrics.

CVSS Score Alone Is Not Sufficient

Raw CVSS scores are calculated for the general software context. For medical devices, you must apply a medical device-specific risk adjustment: (1) Is the vulnerability actually reachable in your device's deployment architecture? (2) Does your device's security architecture (network isolation, authentication) reduce exploitability? (3) What is the patient harm severity if exploited? A CVSS 9.0 vulnerability in a component that is not network-accessible in your device may be effectively CVSS 3.0 in your context. Document this reasoning explicitly — it forms your VEX statement.

// 03 — Response Process

CVE Discovery to Patch Deployment: The Complete Workflow

1

Detection — CVE Identified in SBOM Component

Your SBOM monitoring system (Dependency-Track, Snyk, etc.) alerts that a newly published CVE affects a component in your device SBOM. Initial alert should fire within 24 hours of CVE publication to NVD. Record the CVE ID, CVSS base score, affected component, affected device versions, and affected deployed device count.

2

Triage — Device-Specific Risk Assessment

Within 5 business days: assess whether the vulnerability is reachable and exploitable in your specific device configuration. Use your threat model to evaluate attack path viability. Determine if compensating controls already in place reduce risk. Calculate your device-specific effective CVSS score. Document in a formal vulnerability triage record. Output: controlled risk (proceed to compensating controls documentation) or uncontrolled risk (proceed to patch).

3

Compensating Controls (if Controlled Risk)

If risk is controlled: document the compensating controls in your postmarket file. Issue a customer security advisory describing the vulnerability and recommended network controls. Update your VEX statement in your SBOM. Schedule the vulnerability for remediation in the next planned software update. No FDA reporting required — but document everything.

4

Patch Development and Testing (if Uncontrolled Risk)

Initiate emergency patch development under your IEC 62304 change control process. For Class C software, full regression testing is required before release. For Critical CVSS vulnerabilities (≥9.0), you have 30 days to deploy the patch to affected devices — this includes development, testing, regulatory review, and customer deployment. This timeline is aggressive; having a pre-established rapid-response patch process is essential.

5

Regulatory Reporting

For uncontrolled risk vulnerabilities: notify FDA within 30 days of determining the risk is uncontrolled, per 21 CFR Part 806 (device corrections and removals) if the patch constitutes a correction. For vulnerabilities that could cause or contribute to serious injury or death: evaluate MDR (Medical Device Reporting) obligations under 21 CFR Part 803. For EU-marketed devices: NIS2 requires 24-hour early warning and 72-hour full incident report to national authority if the vulnerability constitutes a security incident affecting patient care.

6

Coordinated Disclosure

Coordinate with CISA ICS-CERT before public vulnerability disclosure. CISA coordinates between the manufacturer, the vulnerability reporter, and the healthcare community. FDA expects manufacturers to lead coordinated disclosure — not to suppress information, but to ensure that disclosure timing does not create risk before patches are available. Issue a public security advisory when the patch is available or compensating controls are defined.

// 04 — Summary

Key Takeaways

30 Days for Critical — Build for It

A 30-day patch cycle for Critical CVEs is extremely aggressive when it includes IEC 62304 change control, regression testing, and customer deployment. Build a pre-established rapid-response process before you need it — not during the first Critical CVE response.

Controlled vs Uncontrolled Is Your Decision Gate

Every vulnerability response begins with the controlled/uncontrolled determination. This is a documented, risk-based decision — not a gut call. Use your ISO 14971 risk estimation methodology and your threat model to justify the determination. If challenged by FDA, this documentation is your defence.

SBOM Monitoring Is the Early Warning System

You cannot respond to vulnerabilities you don't know about. Automated SBOM monitoring against NVD and CISA KEV (Known Exploited Vulnerabilities) feeds is the operational foundation of your postmarket programme. Without it, you will learn about vulnerabilities from customers or FDA — not from your own monitoring.

Coordinate Before You Disclose

Coordinate all public vulnerability disclosures with CISA ICS-CERT. Uncoordinated disclosure of a vulnerability in a life-sustaining device before a patch exists can cause panic, inappropriate device discontinuation by clinical staff, and direct patient harm. CISA exists to manage this coordination — use them.

Next Step Recommendation

Establish your vulnerability response SOP before market entry. Define: who receives CVE alerts, who performs triage, who approves controlled/uncontrolled determinations, who leads patch development, and who notifies FDA and CISA. Run a tabletop exercise with a simulated Critical CVE before your first product launch — the 30-day clock leaves no time to improvise.
