// 01 — Foundation

Why Two Standards? Understanding the Scope Difference

The most common confusion in medical device cybersecurity compliance is between IEC 81001-5-1 and ISO/IEC 27001. Manufacturers frequently assume ISO 27001 certification — which covers organisational information security management — satisfies their medical device product cybersecurity obligations. This assumption is incorrect, and it creates serious regulatory risk.

The fundamental difference is scope: ISO 27001 addresses organisational information security (how you protect your organisation's IT systems and data). IEC 81001-5-1 addresses product cybersecurity (how you design, build, and maintain a secure medical device). Both are necessary — but for different regulatory obligations.

Regulatory Position on IEC 81001-5-1

IEC 81001-5-1:2021 "Health Software and Health IT Systems Safety, Effectiveness and Security — Part 5-1: Security — Activities in the Product Lifecycle" is harmonised by FDA (referenced in FDA 2023 Cyber Guidance as the preferred technical standard), MDCG (referenced in MDCG 2019-16 Cybersecurity Guidance), and TGA. It is the primary device-level standard for medical device cybersecurity worldwide.

// 03 — IEC 81001-5-1 Deep Dive

Key Requirements of IEC 81001-5-1:2021

IEC 81001-5-1 is organised around the security activities that must occur at each phase of the product lifecycle. It is designed to be used alongside IEC 62304 (software lifecycle) and ISO 14971 (risk management) — the three standards together form the complete medical device development framework.

Security Planning (Clause 5.1)

A Security Plan must be developed as part of the overall development plan. It must identify the security environment (intended deployment context, clinical workflow, patient population), define security objectives, and identify applicable standards and regulatory requirements. The Security Plan feeds into the Software Development Plan required by IEC 62304.

Security Risk Management (Clause 5.2)

Cybersecurity risks must be managed within the ISO 14971 risk management framework. Threat modelling (STRIDE) outputs feed into the risk analysis. Security risk controls are treated as risk controls under ISO 14971 — their effectiveness is verified and their residual risk is documented in the risk management report. This integration eliminates the common gap of having separate, inconsistent "cyber risk" and "device risk" analyses.

Secure Design (Clause 5.3)

Security requirements — derived from the threat model and security risk management — must be documented and verifiable. Secure design principles: least privilege, defence in depth, fail-secure, minimal attack surface, separation of privilege. Architecture must be reviewed for security — not just functionality.

Security Verification and Validation (Clause 5.5)

All security requirements must be verified. Testing must include: security functional testing (authentication, authorisation, encryption), vulnerability scanning (SAST, DAST), penetration testing, and fuzz testing. Test results must be documented and traceable to security requirements and threat model entries.

Security Updates (Clause 5.7)

The manufacturer must maintain the capability to deploy security updates throughout the device lifecycle. This includes: maintaining the development environment for released versions, the ability to build and test patches against the original release configuration, and a defined update delivery mechanism for fielded devices.

Security Decommissioning (Clause 5.9)

Security obligations extend to end-of-life. The manufacturer must define a decommissioning process that ensures patient data is securely deleted from device storage, device credentials are revoked, and customers are informed of the end-of-security-support date with sufficient notice to plan replacement.

// 04 — IEC 62443 Context

Where IEC 62443 Fits In

IEC 62443 is the industrial cybersecurity standard for operational technology (OT) and industrial control systems. For medical devices deployed in hospital networks, IEC 62443 becomes relevant when the device interacts with hospital OT infrastructure (building management systems, imaging networks, pharmacy automation).

IEC 62443-4-2: Component Requirements

Technical security requirements for individual device components. Defines Security Levels (SL 1–4) based on attack sophistication. Most connected medical devices target SL 2 (protection against intentional violation using simple means). Referenced by MDCG 2019-16 as a complementary standard alongside IEC 81001-5-1.

IEC 62443 in the Hospital Context

Hospital IT/OT teams use IEC 62443 to evaluate device security claims during procurement. Your device's security architecture documentation (required by FDA) should describe your device's security characteristics in language compatible with IEC 62443 Security Levels — enabling hospital security teams to assess network placement and required controls.

// 05 — Summary

Key Takeaways

ISO 27001 ≠ Product Security

ISO 27001 certifies your organisation's ISMS. It does not certify your product's security. Regulators will not accept ISO 27001 as a substitute for IEC 81001-5-1 compliance in device submissions. You need both — for different purposes.

IEC 81001-5-1 + ISO 14971 Integration

IEC 81001-5-1 is designed to integrate with ISO 14971. Cybersecurity hazards belong in your risk management file. Threat model outputs are risk analysis inputs. Security controls are risk controls. Treating cybersecurity as a separate programme from device risk management creates inconsistency that regulators identify immediately.

Security Decommissioning Is Often Missed

End-of-security-support planning is required by IEC 81001-5-1 but rarely addressed proactively. Define your end-of-support policy before market entry — customers and hospital procurement teams will ask for it, and it must be in your Technical Documentation.

IEC 62443 for Hospital Interoperability

When your device integrates into hospital OT networks, describe your security posture using IEC 62443 Security Level language. Hospital security teams speak this language for procurement evaluation — bridging to it from your IEC 81001-5-1 documentation creates a single, consistent security narrative.
