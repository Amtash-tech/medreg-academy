// 01 — Foundation

The EU Cybersecurity Regulatory Landscape for Medical Devices

EU medical device cybersecurity obligations come from three overlapping regulatory frameworks, each with distinct scope and legal authority. Understanding how they interact is essential before building your EU compliance programme — and the interactions are more complex than the FDA framework.

EU MDR 2017/745 — Annex I GSPR 17.2

General Safety and Performance Requirements mandating minimum IT security measures for devices with an IT security aspect. Applies to all device classes — Class I through III. The primary device-level cybersecurity obligation under EU MDR. Compliance is demonstrated through the Technical Documentation (Annex II) and assessed by Notified Bodies for IIa and above.

EU NIS2 Directive (2022/2555)

Network and Information Security Directive identifying healthcare as an "essential sector." Applies to healthcare providers and device manufacturers meeting size thresholds. Primary obligation: incident reporting — 24-hour early warning and 72-hour full report to national competent authority for significant cybersecurity incidents affecting clinical operations.

EU Cyber Resilience Act (CRA) 2024

Horizontal cybersecurity regulation for all products with digital elements placed on the EU market. Creates mandatory cybersecurity requirements for medical devices that overlap with — but are distinct from — MDR GSPR obligations. Creates a dual-compliance obligation: manufacturers must satisfy both MDR and CRA. Transitional periods apply through 2027.

MDCG 2019-16 Guidance

MDCG "Guidance on Cybersecurity for Medical Devices" (December 2019) provides detailed technical guidance on implementing GSPR 17.2. References IEC 81001-5-1 and IEC 62443 as the primary technical standards. Non-binding but represents Notified Body consensus position — deviating without justification creates audit risk.

// 02 — GSPR 17.2 In Detail

MDR Annex I GSPR 17.2: What Is Required

GSPR 17.2 states that devices incorporating electronic programmable systems or software shall be designed to ensure reproducibility, reliability, and performance — and specifically requires that minimum requirements concerning hardware, IT networks characteristics and IT security measures, including protection against unauthorised access, shall be specified in the instructions for use.

Despite the labelling-focused wording, MDCG 2019-16 clarifies that GSPR 17.2 implies substantive security design obligations — not merely a documentation exercise. The requirements in practice cover:

GSPR 17.2 Applies to ALL Device Classes

Unlike many MDR requirements that scale with device class, GSPR 17.2 applies to all devices with an IT security aspect — including Class I devices. A Class I wellness app that connects to the internet must comply with GSPR 17.2. The depth of evidence required scales with device class — but the obligation exists for all classes with software and connectivity.

// 03 — NIS2 Obligations

NIS2 Directive: Incident Reporting for Medical Device Manufacturers

NIS2 (Directive 2022/2555, transposed into national law across EU member states by October 2024) introduces mandatory incident reporting obligations for healthcare entities and — importantly — for manufacturers supplying healthcare. If your organisation meets the size thresholds (medium enterprise: 50+ employees OR €10M+ annual turnover; large enterprise: 250+ employees OR €50M+ turnover), NIS2 applies.

T+0

Incident Detection

A significant cybersecurity incident is detected — one that causes severe operational disruption to your organisation or to healthcare providers using your device, or that affects the security of network and information systems with potential cross-border impact.

T+24h

Early Warning to National Authority

Within 24 hours of becoming aware of the incident: submit an early warning to your national competent authority (NCA — varies by member state). Content: whether the incident is suspected to be malicious; whether it has or could have cross-border impact. This is a preliminary notification — full information not yet required.

T+72h

Incident Notification

Within 72 hours: submit a full incident notification including initial assessment of severity and impact, indicators of compromise if available, and initial mitigation measures taken. Update the early warning if classification has changed.

T+1 Month

Final Incident Report

Within one month of submitting the incident notification: provide a detailed final report covering full description of the incident, type of threat or root cause, applied and ongoing mitigation measures, and cross-border impact assessment. Regulators use this data for systemic threat analysis across the healthcare sector.

// 04 — Cyber Resilience Act

EU Cyber Resilience Act: Dual Obligation for Medical Device Manufacturers

The EU Cyber Resilience Act (CRA), entered into force October 2024 with transitional periods through 2027, creates mandatory cybersecurity requirements for all products with digital elements placed on the EU market — including medical devices. This creates a dual compliance obligation: MDR + CRA. Meeting MDR GSPR 17.2 alone does not satisfy CRA obligations.

CRA Essential Requirements

Products must: be designed without known exploitable vulnerabilities; have secure default configurations; protect against unauthorised access; protect data integrity and confidentiality; minimise attack surface; support security updates; provide vulnerability disclosure mechanisms; report actively exploited vulnerabilities to ENISA within 24 hours.

CRA vs MDR: Key Differences

CRA is horizontal (all digital products) vs MDR (medical devices only). CRA has its own conformity assessment routes independent of MDR. CRA requires SBOM disclosure to market surveillance authorities on request. CRA has its own vulnerability and incident reporting obligations to ENISA — separate from NIS2 and MDR obligations.

Transition Timelines

CRA vulnerability and incident reporting obligations: September 2026. Full CRA product requirements: December 2027. Products already on market before December 2027 have a grace period — but new products being developed now must be designed for CRA compliance from the outset.

Practical Compliance Integration

Manufacturers with MDR-compliant cybersecurity programmes (IEC 81001-5-1, threat model, SBOM, VDP) will satisfy most CRA technical requirements — the technical substance overlaps significantly. The primary gaps are: CRA-specific conformity assessment documentation, ENISA reporting registration, and CRA labelling requirements.

// 05 — Summary

Key Takeaways

Three EU Frameworks, One Programme

MDR GSPR 17.2, NIS2, and CRA create overlapping but distinct obligations. Build one integrated cybersecurity programme that addresses all three — don't manage them as separate compliance tracks. The technical controls largely overlap; the reporting and documentation requirements differ.

GSPR 17.2 Requires Real Security Design

Despite the labelling-focused wording, GSPR 17.2 requires substantive security design — not just an IFU section. Your Notified Body will expect to see security architecture documentation, threat model, and security testing evidence behind the GSPR 17.2 claim in your Technical Documentation.

NIS2 Timelines Are Short

24 hours for early warning, 72 hours for full notification. In a cybersecurity incident during patient care hours, these timelines will feel impossible without a pre-established incident response plan that includes NIS2 notification as a mandatory step.

Design for CRA Now

CRA product requirements apply from December 2027 — but devices in development now will be on the market then. Design your device to satisfy CRA essential requirements from the start. The incremental cost is far lower than a product redesign to achieve CRA compliance before the deadline.
