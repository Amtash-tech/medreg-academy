// 01 — Foundation

AI/ML in Medical Devices: The Regulatory Landscape

Artificial intelligence and machine learning in medical devices present unique regulatory challenges because AI/ML systems can change their behavior over time, make decisions that are difficult to interpret, and may perform differently across patient populations. Regulators worldwide are developing frameworks to ensure these systems remain safe throughout their lifecycle.

The global regulatory approach is converging around three principles: transparent performance (know how well the system works and for whom), representative data (train and validate on populations reflecting real use), and continuous monitoring (verify real-world performance matches pre-market claims).

Locked vs. Adaptive Algorithms

Locked: Algorithm parameters fixed at deployment — any change requires regulatory review or approved PCCP. Adaptive: Algorithm continues learning post-deployment. Adaptive algorithms face the highest regulatory scrutiny as performance can change without manufacturer action.

Why AI Needs Special Rules

Traditional device risk management assumes design is fixed. AI/ML systems can "drift" — their real-world performance may change as clinical practice, equipment, or patient populations evolve. This requires lifecycle-long performance monitoring, not just pre-market validation.

FDA's AI/ML Action Plan (2021)

FDA's five-part action plan: (1) develop PCCP framework, (2) good machine learning practice, (3) patient-centered approach, (4) algorithm transparency, (5) real-world performance monitoring. This plan underpins all current FDA AI/ML regulatory activity.

Global Convergence via GMLP

FDA, Health Canada, and UK MHRA jointly issued 10 GMLP (Good Machine Learning Practice) principles in 2021. This represents significant international harmonization — manufacturers building to GMLP principles are simultaneously meeting expectations across three major jurisdictions.

// 02 — GMLP Principles

Good Machine Learning Practice (GMLP)

The 10 GMLP principles, jointly issued by FDA, Health Canada, and UK MHRA, define foundational best practices for AI/ML medical device development. These principles are not just guidance — they represent the minimum expected standard for responsible AI in healthcare.

01

Multi-Disciplinary Expertise

Teams developing and deploying AI/ML-enabled devices encompass diverse expertise — clinical, data science, regulatory, software engineering, and ethics.

02

Good Software Engineering

Software engineering and security practices are applied to ML methodology, including version control, testing, code review, and documentation per IEC 62304.

03

Clinical Study Design

Data collection for clinical studies is designed to support development and evaluation of AI/ML algorithms for their intended clinical use. Relevance of intended use patient population ensured from design.

04

Training Data Independence

Training and test datasets are separated and representative of the intended patient population. Data management allows for traceability and integrity throughout the lifecycle.

05

Appropriate Reference Datasets

Selected reference datasets are based on best available methods and representative of the intended population, including relevant subgroups (demographic, clinical, technical).

06

Model Design Transparency

The AI/ML model is designed to achieve the stated goal while managing risks in the context of the device's intended use. Model design choices are documented and justified.

07

Human-AI Team Performance

The performance evaluation should account for the human-AI team — how clinicians will interact with and act on the AI output. The AI doesn't act alone.

08

Testing on Independent Data

Testing is performed on independent test sets representing the intended use population, including challenging and edge cases. Performance is reported with appropriate statistics and uncertainty.

09

Revealed Limitations

Users are provided with relevant information to use the AI/ML device appropriately — including known limitations, appropriate use cases, and scenarios where performance may be reduced.

10

Monitoring in Production

Deployed AI/ML models are monitored for performance in the real world. Plans are in place to address performance issues including performance drift, unexpected failures, and bias emergence.

// 03 — Dataset Requirements

Dataset Governance and Requirements

Dataset quality is the most critical factor in AI/ML SaMD safety. Regulatory expectations for datasets are increasingly specific — reflecting hard lessons from AI systems that performed poorly for underrepresented populations.

1

Training / Validation / Test Set Separation

Three independent datasets must be maintained. Training: used to fit model parameters. Validation: used during development to tune hyperparameters and prevent overfitting. Test: completely locked, unseen data used only for final performance reporting. No overlap between sets — data leakage is a critical regulatory concern.

2

Representativeness Assessment

Datasets must represent the intended use population. This requires documenting: demographic composition (age, sex, race/ethnicity), clinical characteristics (disease severity, comorbidities), technical characteristics (imaging equipment manufacturers, acquisition protocols, institution types). Subgroup performance must be evaluated and reported.

3

Data Quality and Provenance

Document the source of all training and test data, labeling methodology (who labeled, what criteria, inter-rater reliability), data cleaning procedures, exclusion criteria, and any known limitations. EU AI Act Article 10 requires data governance documentation as a standalone requirement.

4

Performance Metric Selection

Choose performance metrics appropriate to the clinical task. For classification: sensitivity, specificity, AUC-ROC, PPV, NPV. For detection: FROC curves. Report metrics with confidence intervals and statistical significance. FDA expects performance to be reported per intended use subpopulations.

Bias Analysis and Mitigation

Algorithmic bias in medical AI can cause systematic harm to underserved populations. Regulatory authorities now explicitly require bias analysis and mitigation as a core component of AI/ML submissions.

Types of AI Bias in Medical Devices

Training data bias: Underrepresentation of demographic groups. Label bias: Systematic errors in ground truth labels. Evaluation bias: Test set not reflecting real-world population. Deployment bias: Device used in populations not represented in training.

EU AI Act Art. 10 Data Governance

High-risk AI systems must have training, validation, and testing datasets examined for biases — particularly those relevant to the intended purpose. Data governance practices must be documented. Special categories of personal data may only be used when strictly necessary and under specific conditions.

Required Bias Analysis Documentation

FDA expects: description of how the dataset was assembled to be representative, analysis of performance by demographic subgroup, identification of known performance gaps, and disclosure of limitations in labeling/IFU. MDCG 2025-6 has similar requirements for MDAI.

Mitigation Strategies

Data augmentation and oversampling of underrepresented groups. Targeted data collection from underrepresented sites. Subgroup-specific performance thresholds. Labeling restrictions to exclude populations where bias cannot be mitigated. Post-market monitoring by subgroup.

// 04 — Algorithm Change Control

Predetermined Change Control Plan (PCCP)

The PCCP is FDA's solution to a fundamental tension in AI regulation: algorithms need to improve over time, but every change to a cleared/approved device traditionally requires a new submission. A PCCP pre-approves a set of anticipated changes, enabling iterative improvement without regulatory burden for each update.

What a PCCP Must Include

(1) Description of modifications: Specific changes anticipated — retraining with new data, adding new intended uses, architecture changes. (2) Modification protocol: Methodology for making, validating, and implementing each change type. (3) Impact assessment: How each change affects safety and effectiveness.

Performance Criteria in PCCP

For each anticipated modification, the PCCP must specify pre-defined performance thresholds. The device can only be updated if the new version meets or exceeds these criteria on the independent test set. Failure to meet criteria triggers escalation — typically requiring a new submission.

What Requires a New Submission

Changes outside the approved PCCP scope. Performance degradation below PCCP thresholds. New intended uses not covered in the PCCP. Changes to the device's risk class. Any modification not covered by the approved change protocol.

EU Equivalent: Change Management

EU MDR and AI Act do not have an exact PCCP equivalent but require change management processes. Under MDCG 2025-6: significant modifications to AI may require new conformity assessment. Health Canada is actively aligning with FDA PCCP framework.

PCCP is Not a Blank Check

FDA's PCCP does not permit unlimited algorithm changes. Each change type must be specifically described in advance, with defined validation methodology and performance criteria. The PCCP is a negotiated framework — manufacturers must propose it, and FDA must agree to its scope during clearance/approval review.

// 05 — EU AI Act

EU AI Act and Medical Device AI

The EU AI Act (2024) is the world's first comprehensive AI regulation. For medical device AI, it creates a dual-compliance requirement: medical device AI systems (MDAI) that qualify as high-risk under EU AI Act Annex III must comply with BOTH EU MDR/IVDR AND EU AI Act requirements.

MDCG 2025-6: The Dual Compliance Framework

MDCG 2025-6 (April 2025) provides the practical guidance for manufacturers navigating dual MDR/AI Act compliance. Key principle: where the AI Act adds requirements beyond MDR (data governance, bias analysis, transparency, human oversight), these are additive obligations — the MDR/IVDR requirements remain fully applicable and are not replaced.

// 06 — Post-Market Monitoring

AI/ML Post-Market Performance Monitoring

Post-market monitoring for AI/ML SaMD is required by both FDA and EU regulators — and is more complex than for traditional devices because AI performance can change without any deliberate modification to the device.

Performance Drift Monitoring

Even locked algorithms must be monitored for performance drift — gradual degradation caused by changes in clinical practice, patient population, imaging technology, or data collection methods. Statistical process control methods can detect early signs of drift before patient harm occurs.

Real-World vs. Pre-Market Performance

Pre-market test set performance may not reflect real-world performance if the test set was not truly representative. Post-market monitoring is the only way to confirm real-world performance. GMLP Principle 10 requires plans for this monitoring to be in place before deployment.

Subgroup Monitoring

Post-market monitoring should track performance by relevant subgroups — demographic, clinical, technical. Emergent bias (bias not present at launch but developing as the deployment context changes) is a known risk in medical AI that only post-market monitoring can detect.

Regulatory Reporting Triggers

Under FDA: performance degradation meeting MDR reporting thresholds triggers adverse event reporting obligations. Under EU MDR: performance changes must be reported through PMS/PSUR system. Under EU AI Act Article 72: post-market monitoring results feed into continuous AI risk management.
