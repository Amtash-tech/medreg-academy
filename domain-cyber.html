// 01 — Foundation

Cybersecurity as a Total Product Lifecycle Obligation

FDA's cybersecurity framework for medical devices is built on the principle of Total Product Lifecycle (TPLC) security — cybersecurity is not a checkbox at premarket submission but a continuous engineering and management discipline from design through decommissioning.

The Consolidated Appropriations Act (2023) granted FDA explicit authority to require cybersecurity information in premarket submissions and to refuse to accept applications that do not include required cybersecurity documentation. This represents a fundamental shift from guidance-based expectations to statutory requirements.

Why Cybersecurity Matters for Devices

Connected medical devices — infusion pumps, pacemaker programmers, imaging systems, SaMD — can be attacked to alter therapy delivery, exfiltrate patient data, or disrupt clinical operations. A successful attack can directly cause patient harm.

FDA's Statutory Authority (2023)

Section 524B of the FD&C Act (added by CARES Act 2023) requires manufacturers of cyber devices to submit a cybersecurity plan, SBOM, and vulnerability disclosure policy. FDA can refuse to file submissions lacking these elements.

"Cyber Device" Definition

FDA defines a "cyber device" as a device that includes software, is intended to connect to the internet, and contains technological characteristics that could be vulnerable to cybersecurity threats. Most modern SaMD and connected devices qualify.

TPLC Security Principles

Security must be designed in from the start (Security by Design), monitored continuously post-market, and updated throughout the device lifecycle. Patching and vulnerability response are permanent obligations, not optional post-market activities.

// 02 — Premarket Requirements

FDA Premarket Cybersecurity Submission Requirements

FDA's 2023 final guidance "Cybersecurity in Medical Devices: Quality System Considerations and Content of Premarket Submissions" specifies what cybersecurity documentation must be included in 510(k), PMA, De Novo, and HDE submissions.

1

Cybersecurity Management Plan

A plan demonstrating how the manufacturer will monitor, identify, and address post-market cybersecurity vulnerabilities and exploits. Must include processes for coordinated vulnerability disclosure, timelines for patch deployment, and criteria for determining when an update is required.

2

Software Bill of Materials (SBOM)

A machine-readable, comprehensive inventory of all software components — commercial, open-source, and custom — including version information and known vulnerabilities. Minimum data fields per NTIA guidance. Required to enable rapid CVE assessment.

3

Threat Model

A structured analysis of the device's cybersecurity threat landscape — identifying assets, threat actors, attack surfaces, potential threats, and implemented mitigations. STRIDE, PASTA, or equivalent framework acceptable. Must be documented and linked to security architecture.

4

Security Architecture Documentation

Diagrams and descriptions of the device's security architecture — data flows, network interfaces, authentication mechanisms, encryption implementation, and security controls. Shows how the design mitigates identified threats.

5

Cybersecurity Testing Evidence

Evidence from security testing — including static analysis (SAST), dynamic analysis (DAST), fuzzing, and penetration testing. Documentation of discovered vulnerabilities and how they were resolved or risk-accepted with rationale.

6

Vulnerability Disclosure Policy

A publicly accessible policy describing how the manufacturer will receive, acknowledge, and respond to vulnerability reports from external researchers, customers, and coordinated disclosure partners (e.g., CISA ICS-CERT).

SBOM: Software Bill of Materials

An SBOM is the cybersecurity equivalent of a device's bill of materials. It enables rapid identification of affected components when new CVEs are published. FDA requires machine-readable format (SPDX or CycloneDX recommended).

Required SBOM Elements

Component name and version, supplier/author name, unique identifier, dependency relationship, cryptographic hash (recommended), timestamp of last update, license information for open-source components.

SBOM Maintenance

The SBOM must be kept current throughout the device lifecycle — updated whenever software components change. Manufacturers must monitor CVE databases (NVD) against SBOM entries and assess impact within defined timeframes.

// 03 — Postmarket Management

Postmarket Cybersecurity Vulnerability Management

FDA's postmarket cybersecurity guidance establishes that manufacturers are responsible for continuously monitoring their marketed devices for cybersecurity vulnerabilities and responding in a risk-proportionate manner.

Controlled vs Uncontrolled Risk

Controlled risk: Vulnerability can be adequately mitigated through compensating controls without deploying a patch (e.g., network isolation, user guidance). Uncontrolled risk: Compensating controls insufficient — patch must be deployed within defined timelines.

Coordinated Vulnerability Disclosure

Manufacturers must have a process where external researchers can privately report vulnerabilities. Manufacturer has a defined window to develop a fix before public disclosure. FDA and CISA support coordinated disclosure frameworks.

MDR Reporting Trigger

Cybersecurity incidents that cause or contribute to serious injury or death must be reported to FDA under 21 CFR Part 803 MDR requirements — just like any other device malfunction. Cybersecurity is not exempt from adverse event reporting.

End-of-Life Obligations

When a device reaches end of software support (EOSS), manufacturers must notify customers and provide guidance on risk mitigation. Legacy devices with unmitigated critical vulnerabilities may require recall or patient notification.

The "Unacceptable Patient Risk" Threshold

FDA's postmarket guidance defines that manufacturers must act when a vulnerability poses unacceptable risk to patient safety. Factors include: exploitability of the vulnerability, attack complexity, potential patient harm severity, and availability of compensating controls. Documentation of this risk assessment is required even for accepted risks.

// 04 — EU Requirements

EU Cybersecurity Requirements

EU MDR requires cybersecurity through Annex I GSPR §17.2, which mandates that electronic programmable systems and software be designed to protect against unauthorized access and to ensure the security, integrity, and availability of data. MDCG cybersecurity guidance elaborates on these requirements.

MDR Annex I GSPR 17.2

Requirements for electronic devices: protection against unauthorized access, secure interfaces, protection of software integrity, and measures ensuring system availability. Minimum IT security measures must be documented in labeling/IFU for all MDSW, including Class I.

NIS2 Directive (Healthcare)

EU NIS2 (2022/2555) designates healthcare as an "essential sector." Healthcare entities must implement cybersecurity risk management and report significant cybersecurity incidents to national authorities within 24 hours (early warning) and 72 hours (full report).

MDCG Cybersecurity Guidance

Maps EU MDR GSPR requirements to specific security controls. Requires security risk assessment integrated with ISO 14971 risk management. Addresses state-of-the-art security measures, access control, data protection, and update mechanisms.

EU AI Act + Cybersecurity

EU AI Act Article 15 requires high-risk AI systems (including MDAI) to be resilient against attempts to alter their use, outputs, or performance by third parties. MDCG 2025-6 requires cybersecurity testing specifically for AI model security, not just traditional software security.
