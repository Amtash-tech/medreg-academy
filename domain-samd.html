// 01 — Foundation

What is SaMD?

Software as a Medical Device (SaMD) is defined by IMDRF N10:2013 as "software intended to be used for one or more medical purposes that perform these purposes without being part of a hardware medical device." This definition is the globally adopted baseline used by FDA, EU, Health Canada, TGA, and MHRA.

SaMD is distinct from Software IN a Medical Device (SiMD) — software that is embedded in or controls a hardware medical device. Understanding this distinction is critical because it determines which regulatory framework applies to your product.

SaMD Examples

Mobile apps that analyze ECG data for arrhythmia detection. Cloud-based imaging algorithms that detect diabetic retinopathy. Clinical decision support software flagging sepsis risk. Software predicting patient deterioration from vital signs.

SiMD Examples

Firmware embedded in an infusion pump controlling drug delivery. Software driving the imaging acquisition in an MRI scanner. Pacemaker algorithm software controlling cardiac stimulation. Ventilator control software managing respiratory therapy.

Non-Device Software

Administrative hospital management software. General wellness apps (step counters, meditation). Software merely storing/transmitting clinical data without interpreting it. CDS meeting all four FDA non-device criteria under 21st Century Cures Act.

The MDSW Qualification Test (EU)

EU MDR adds a qualification step per MDCG 2021-24: Does the software have a medical purpose AND does it create or modify medical information (vs. merely store/display/transmit it)? Both must be true to qualify as MDSW subject to CE marking.

Key Regulatory Principle

The intended use of the software — not its technical architecture — determines whether it is a medical device. Software with identical technology may or may not be regulated depending entirely on the manufacturer's intended medical purpose claims in labeling and marketing.

// 02 — Risk Classification

Risk Classification Frameworks

Risk classification drives every subsequent regulatory decision — submission pathway, required evidence, Notified Body involvement, and post-market obligations. Three major frameworks apply globally.

IMDRF N12 Risk Categories

The IMDRF N12:2014 framework uses two dimensions: (1) significance of information provided by SaMD to the healthcare decision, and (2) state of the healthcare situation. The intersection determines the risk category (I through IV).

FDA Classification System

FDA classifies devices into three classes. For SaMD, classification is determined by the device type's product code in the FDA classification database. The risk-based class determines the required premarket pathway.

Class I — General Controls

Lowest risk. Most Class I devices exempt from premarket notification. Requires facility registration, device listing, and QSR compliance. Examples: some fitness/wellness tracking software with minor medical claims.

Class II — Special Controls

Moderate risk. Requires 510(k) premarket notification (or De Novo for novel devices). Special controls (performance standards, post-market surveillance) supplement general controls. Most SaMD falls here.

Class III — PMA Required

Highest risk. Life-sustaining or life-supporting software, implanted devices, novel high-risk uses. Requires Premarket Approval with clinical studies demonstrating safety and effectiveness. Most stringent pathway.

CDS Exemption

Under 21st Century Cures Act: CDS meeting all four criteria (not for serious/critical decisions, displays basis, allows independent review, clinician acts as intermediary) is exempt from device regulation entirely.

EU MDR Rule 11 Classification

EU MDR Rule 11 applies specifically to software. Classification depends on the intended purpose of the software and the potential consequences of incorrect information on patient health.

// 03 — Software Lifecycle

Software Development Lifecycle (IEC 62304)

IEC 62304 is the core international standard for medical device software lifecycle processes. It defines activities for software development, maintenance, risk management integration, and configuration management. The standard applies to both SaMD and SiMD and is required or strongly expected by FDA, EU MDR, Health Canada, and TGA.

IEC 62304 assigns three Software Safety Classes (A, B, C) based on the severity of potential harm from software failure. The safety class determines the required rigor of development activities.

Class A — No Injury

Software failure cannot contribute to a hazardous situation. Minimal lifecycle requirements. Basic documentation, maintenance, and change management required.

Class B — Non-Serious Injury

Software failure may contribute to a hazardous situation but only with non-serious injury potential. Moderate lifecycle requirements including unit testing and architecture documentation.

Class C — Serious Injury / Death

Software failure could result in serious injury or death. Full lifecycle requirements: detailed design, unit testing, integration testing, system testing, traceability from requirements through testing.

SOUP (Software of Unknown Provenance)

Third-party software, open-source libraries, and COTS components are treated as SOUP. IEC 62304 requires identification, documented requirements for use, hazard analysis, and testing of all SOUP items.

1

Software Development Planning

Define software development lifecycle model, standards to be used, activities, deliverables, and how IEC 62304 requirements will be satisfied. Risk management integration planning per ISO 14971.

2

Software Requirements Analysis

Define and document functional, performance, interface, and safety/security requirements. Requirements must be traceable through design, implementation, and testing.

3

Software Architectural Design

Identify software items, their interfaces, and safety class assignment. Architecture must be documented and reviewed. SOUP items identified and assessed.

4

Detailed Design, Implementation & Verification

Detailed design of software units. Coding to documented standards. Unit testing and integration testing per safety class requirements. Verification that outputs meet inputs.

5

Software System Testing & Release

System-level testing against software requirements. Regression testing. Documentation of known anomalies. Release procedures and configuration management. Software maintenance planning.

// 04 — Clinical Evidence

Clinical Evaluation (IMDRF N41)

Clinical evaluation for SaMD follows the IMDRF N41:2017 framework, adopted by FDA, TGA, Health Canada, and MHRA. It defines three building blocks that must each be established with appropriate clinical evidence.

BB1

Valid Clinical Association

Establish that the relationship between the SaMD output and the targeted clinical condition is clinically meaningful and scientifically valid. Based on published literature, physiology, or new research. This is the foundation — without it, the SaMD has no clinical rationale.

BB2

Analytical Validation

Demonstrate that the SaMD correctly and accurately processes input data to generate reliable, precise output data. Includes testing the algorithm/model on representative data. For AI/ML: performance metrics on an independent locked test set (sensitivity, specificity, AUC, PPV, NPV).

BB3

Clinical Validation

Demonstrate that the SaMD's output leads to accurate diagnoses, informed treatment decisions, or improved health outcomes in the intended clinical context. May require clinical studies, retrospective data analysis, or post-market clinical follow-up depending on risk class.

EU MDR Requirement

EU MDR requires a formal Clinical Evaluation Report (CER) per Annex XIV — a structured document covering literature review, clinical data analysis, benefit-risk assessment, and SOTA review. The CER must be continuously updated with post-market clinical data throughout the device's lifecycle.

// 05 — Regulatory Pathways

FDA Regulatory Pathways for SaMD

510(k) Premarket Notification

Demonstrates substantial equivalence to a legally marketed predicate device. Most common SaMD pathway. Requires software documentation, predicate comparison, and performance data. No clinical study typically required unless needed for equivalence.

De Novo Request

For novel, low-to-moderate risk SaMD with no valid predicate. Creates a new classification with special controls. Future devices can use the De Novo as a 510(k) predicate. Requires safety/effectiveness demonstration and proposed special controls.

Premarket Approval (PMA)

For Class III, highest-risk SaMD. Requires valid scientific evidence — typically pivotal clinical studies — demonstrating reasonable assurance of safety and effectiveness. Most rigorous pathway with highest evidentiary bar.

Pre-Submission (Q-Sub)

Not a submission pathway but a critical early-stage tool. Allows manufacturers to get FDA feedback on classification, clinical study design, and submission requirements before investing in studies. Strongly recommended for novel or complex SaMD.

AI/ML SaMD: The PCCP Approach

For AI/ML SaMD that will require algorithm updates over time, FDA's Predetermined Change Control Plan (PCCP) allows pre-approval of anticipated algorithm modifications. With an approved PCCP, qualifying algorithm updates can be deployed without requiring a new 510(k) submission — enabling iterative AI improvement while maintaining regulatory compliance.
