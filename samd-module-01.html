// 01 — Foundation

Why IMDRF N12 Exists

Before IMDRF N12:2014, regulators globally had no consistent framework for categorising Software as a Medical Device by risk. A diabetic decision-support app and an ICU ventilator controller might both be called "SaMD" — but they carry completely different patient risk profiles and should face proportionately different regulatory scrutiny.

IMDRF N12 solves this by providing a two-axis risk categorization matrix that is jurisdiction-neutral. It does not create regulatory classes on its own — instead, it provides the global baseline that national regulators (FDA, MDR, Health Canada, TGA) then map to their own classification systems. Understanding N12 is therefore the prerequisite for understanding every SaMD regulatory pathway worldwide.

Regulatory Status of N12

IMDRF N12:2014 is non-binding guidance — it does not carry the force of law in any jurisdiction. However, FDA's SaMD guidance, EU MDCG 2021-24, Health Canada, and TGA all explicitly reference or align to the N12 framework. In practice, regulators expect manufacturers to articulate their SaMD risk category using N12 terminology.

// 02 — First Axis

Axis 1: Significance of the Information Provided by SaMD

The first axis asks: what is the healthcare decision being driven by the SaMD output? IMDRF N12 defines three levels of output significance, ranked by the directness of the link between the software output and the clinical action taken:

Significance LevelDefinitionExamples
Treat / Diagnose SaMD output directly drives treatment or diagnosis decisions with no intermediary clinical assessment Closed-loop insulin delivery algorithm; AI-automated cancer diagnosis without radiologist review
Drive Clinical Management Output directly drives next clinical management step — clinician acts on the output but uses clinical judgment to execute Sepsis alert triggering immediate ICU transfer; triage scoring system directing care pathway
Inform Clinical Management Output provides information to support clinical decisions — clinician independently evaluates before acting Vital sign trending dashboard; population health risk stratification tool; medication adherence tracker

The Human Intermediary Question

The critical distinction between "Drive" and "Inform" is who bears the immediate decision burden. If a clinician sees the output and must independently verify or reason before acting, it is "Inform." If the output directly triggers the action with the clinician primarily executing rather than deciding, it is "Drive." Under MDCG 2021-24 (EU), the presence of a human intermediary reviewing output does NOT automatically reduce significance — the intermediary must be genuinely assessing, not merely ratifying.

// 03 — Second Axis

Axis 2: Severity of the Healthcare Situation or Condition

The second axis asks: what is the severity of the patient's condition or the healthcare situation in which the SaMD is intended to be used? This is not about the severity of potential software harm — it is about the baseline clinical acuity of the intended use population.

Situation SeverityDefinitionExamples
Critical Conditions that are immediately life-threatening or irreversible without immediate intervention Acute MI, septic shock, respiratory failure, stroke, severe trauma
Serious Conditions that may cause permanent impairment or require major therapeutic intervention if not treated Uncontrolled diabetes, cardiac arrhythmia, moderate depression requiring medication
Non-Serious Conditions not likely to result in harm even without intervention; general wellness or minor conditions Seasonal allergies, mild fatigue tracking, general fitness monitoring

Key Clarification: Intended Use Population

The severity assessment is based on the intended use population, not the worst-case patient who might use the device. If your SaMD is intended for general wellness monitoring of healthy adults, Axis 2 is Non-Serious — even if a patient with an undiagnosed condition happens to use it.

// 05 — Categories

Categories I–IV: Regulatory Implications

Category I — Low Risk

SaMD that informs clinical management in non-serious conditions. Regulatory burden is lowest. FDA maps to Class I (general controls, typically exempt from premarket notification). EU MDR Rule 11 typically Class I (self-certification). Example: A wellness app tracking hydration levels for healthy adults. Practical note: Even Category I SaMD must comply with basic QMS requirements and post-market surveillance obligations under ISO 13485 and applicable national law.

Category II — Medium Risk

SaMD informing clinical management in serious conditions, or driving clinical management in non-serious conditions. FDA maps to Class II (510(k) clearance typical). EU MDR Rule 11 typically Class IIa. Example: A medication adherence tracker for diabetic patients; a heart rate alert system for cardiac rehabilitation. Key requirement: Predicate identification strategy becomes critical for FDA 510(k). Full IEC 62304 compliance required.

Category III — High Risk

SaMD treating/diagnosing in non-serious or serious conditions; driving clinical management in serious or critical conditions; informing clinical management in critical conditions. FDA maps to Class II (510(k) or De Novo) or Class III (PMA). EU MDR Rule 11 typically Class IIb. Example: A CAD detection AI requiring radiologist review; a sepsis scoring system triggering care escalation. Key requirement: Clinical evidence under IMDRF N41 becomes substantive. Pre-Submission meeting with FDA strongly recommended.

Category IV — Critical Risk

SaMD treating/diagnosing or driving clinical management in critical conditions. Highest regulatory burden. FDA maps to Class III (PMA required unless De Novo available for novel low-to-moderate risk device). EU MDR Rule 11 typically Class III (full Notified Body assessment). Example: Autonomous closed-loop insulin delivery; AI-driven diagnosis of acute MI for immediate catheterization decision. Key requirement: Randomised controlled trial or equivalent clinical evidence often required. PMCF plan mandatory from day one.

// 07 — Common Pitfalls

The Most Common Classification Errors

Confusing Device Harm with Situation Severity

Axis 2 is about the patient's condition, not the potential harm from the software. A wellness app used by a patient who turns out to be critically ill is still Non-Serious on Axis 2 — because the intended use is general wellness. The software's failure consequences are addressed in ISO 14971 risk management, not in N12 categorization.

Misclassifying "Inform" vs "Drive"

Manufacturers frequently downgrade Axis 1 to "Inform" by adding a disclaimer that a clinician reviews the output. This is invalid if the output directly determines the next clinical step. Regulators assess the actual clinical workflow, not the label. If the clinician's "review" is effectively rubber-stamping the output, it is "Drive."

Forgetting N12 Maps to National Classes

N12 categories (I–IV) are not the same as FDA classes (I–III) or EU MDR classes (I, IIa, IIb, III). The mapping is approximate and jurisdiction-specific. Category II N12 can map to Class IIa EU MDR or Class II FDA — but always verify against the specific national classification rules (Rule 11 for EU MDR; 21 CFR Part 892 etc. for FDA).

Treating N12 as Sufficient for EU Classification

In the EU, SaMD classification under MDR 2017/745 is determined by Annex VIII classification rules — primarily Rule 11 for software, supplemented by MDCG 2021-24 guidance. N12 is an input to this process, not a substitute. Always run the MDCG 2021-24 two-step MDSW qualification test before applying Rule 11.

// 08 — Summary

Key Takeaways

Two Axes, One Matrix

Every SaMD risk categorization under N12 requires two independent assessments: the significance of the software output (Treat/Drive/Inform) and the severity of the intended healthcare situation (Critical/Serious/Non-Serious).

Category ≠ Regulatory Class

IMDRF N12 categories (I–IV) are a global reference framework. Each jurisdiction maps them differently. Always cross-reference with FDA guidance, EU MDR Rule 11, or applicable national rules before finalizing classification.

Human Intermediary Doesn't Automatically Help

Claiming a clinician reviews the output does not reduce classification unless the clinician is genuinely independently assessing — not just acting on — the software output. This is the single most litigated classification question in SaMD submissions.

Higher Category = Earlier Regulatory Engagement

For Category III and IV SaMD, engage regulators early. FDA's Pre-Submission (Q-Sub) program exists precisely for this. EU manufacturers should engage a Notified Body before finalizing the technical file approach for Class IIb and III devices.

Next Step Recommendation

After determining your N12 category: for FDA submissions, map to FDA device class and identify the appropriate premarket pathway (Module 02 covers the 510(k) process in detail). For EU submissions, run the MDCG 2021-24 MDSW qualification test and apply Rule 11. For novel SaMD with no clear predicate, consider FDA De Novo (Module 06 covers pathway selection).

Document your N12 categorization rationale in writing — it will be expected in both FDA submissions and EU Technical Documentation as part of the Intended Purpose and Device Description.
