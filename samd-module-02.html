// 01 — Foundation

What Is the 510(k) Pathway?

Section 510(k) of the Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act requires device manufacturers to notify FDA at least 90 days before marketing a device. For SaMD, this is the most commonly used premarket pathway — applicable to Class II devices that can demonstrate substantial equivalence to a legally marketed predicate device.

The 510(k) does not require proof that your device is safe and effective on its own. Instead, it requires proof that your device is as safe and effective as a predicate — a device already legally marketed in the US before May 28, 1976, or cleared through a prior 510(k) or De Novo. This distinction is fundamental: you are not running clinical trials to prove efficacy, you are demonstrating comparability.

The Substantial Equivalence Standard (21 CFR 807.87)

Substantial equivalence requires: (1) the same intended use as the predicate, AND (2) the same technological characteristics — OR different technological characteristics that do not raise new questions of safety/effectiveness and perform at least as well as the predicate. Both conditions must be met. Intended use is evaluated first; technology second.

// 02 — Predicate Strategy

Identifying and Selecting a Predicate Device

Predicate selection is the most strategically important decision in a 510(k) submission. A poor predicate choice — even a technically correct one — can lead to an RTQ (Request to Provide Additional Information) or NSE (Not Substantially Equivalent) determination.

1

Search the 510(k) Database (CDRH 510(k))

Use FDA's 510(k) Premarket Notification database at accessdata.fda.gov. Search by product code, device name, and intended use terms. Your product code determines the regulatory classification, applicable performance standards, and Special Controls.

2

Evaluate Intended Use Match

The predicate's intended use must match yours. "Intended use" for SaMD includes: the medical condition or disease; the patient population; the clinical workflow context; and the specific function the software performs. A radiology CAD device cannot be used as a predicate for a cardiology AI tool even if the underlying algorithm is similar.

3

Assess Technological Characteristics

For software: compare algorithm type, operating environment, user interface paradigm, data inputs, and output format. If your device uses machine learning and the predicate uses rule-based algorithms, this constitutes different technological characteristics — you must then demonstrate the differences do not raise new safety questions.

4

Split Predicate Strategy

If no single predicate covers your full device, FDA permits a split predicate — using one predicate for intended use and a second for technological characteristics. This is accepted practice but must be explicitly disclosed and justified in the submission.

// 04 — Process & Timeline

The 510(k) Review Process

Acceptance Review (15 days)

FDA checks whether the submission is administratively complete — not scientific merit. Missing mandatory sections result in Refuse to Accept (RTA). Common RTA reasons: missing intended use statement, missing predicate table, missing software documentation, incomplete labelling.

Substantive Review (90 days total)

FDA reviewer assesses substantial equivalence. The 90-day clock resets when FDA sends an Additional Information (AI) request. Responding promptly and completely is critical — a second AI request significantly delays clearance. Average FDA 510(k) review time for SaMD is 120–180 days including AI interactions.

Interactive Review

FDA encourages interactive review for complex submissions. This means telephonic discussions with the reviewer before the formal AI letter is issued — saving time by resolving questions informally. Request interactive review in your cover letter.

Pre-Submission (Q-Sub) Meeting

For novel SaMD, a Pre-Submission meeting before filing allows you to get FDA feedback on your predicate strategy, testing approach, and labelling before investing in full submission preparation. FDA responds in writing within 70 days of submission. This is the single highest-ROI activity in SaMD regulatory strategy.

When 510(k) Is Not Appropriate

If your SaMD has no valid predicate (novel technology or novel intended use), 510(k) is not available. You must use either De Novo (for novel low-to-moderate risk SaMD — Module 06 covers pathway selection) or PMA (for Class III). Do not attempt to force a 510(k) with a poor predicate — NSE determinations are publicly documented and can harm your regulatory record.

// 05 — Summary

Key Takeaways

Predicate First

The 510(k) strategy lives or dies on predicate selection. Identify your predicate before writing anything else. A strong, well-matched predicate makes every other section easier to write and defend.

Substantial Equivalence ≠ Identical

Different technological characteristics are acceptable if they don't raise new safety questions. AI/ML algorithms routinely differ from rule-based predicates — you must characterise the difference and provide performance data showing at least equivalent safety and effectiveness.

Software Documentation Is Non-Negotiable

IEC 62304 lifecycle documentation, SOUP lists, and V&V evidence are mandatory for any SaMD 510(k). FDA will issue RTA for missing software sections. Plan 3–6 months of software documentation work alongside product development.

Pre-Sub Saves Months

For any non-trivial SaMD 510(k), a Pre-Submission meeting is not optional — it is a risk mitigation tool. The 70-day wait for a Pre-Sub response is far cheaper than a 510(k) NSE determination.

Next Step Recommendation

Immediately after determining 510(k) is appropriate: (1) Identify and lock your predicate strategy, (2) Request a Pre-Submission meeting if any uncertainty exists about predicate choice or testing approach, (3) Initiate IEC 62304 software lifecycle documentation — this cannot be completed retroactively. Module 03 covers EU MDR Rule 11 classification, which runs in parallel for dual-market strategies.
