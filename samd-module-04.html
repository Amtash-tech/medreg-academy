// 01 — Foundation

Clinical Evaluation for SaMD: Why It's Different

Clinical evaluation for SaMD is fundamentally different from clinical evaluation for physical devices. A physical device's safety is largely determined by biocompatibility, mechanical testing, and sterility. For SaMD, safety and effectiveness depend on whether the software's outputs correctly reflect clinical reality — and whether acting on those outputs produces the intended clinical benefit.

IMDRF N41:2017 "Software as a Medical Device: Clinical Evaluation" provides the internationally recognised framework. It is adopted by FDA as a reference, and underpins the EU MDR Clinical Evaluation requirements (Annex XIV) for SaMD. The framework is built on three sequential building blocks that must all be established.

// 02 — The Three Building Blocks

IMDRF N41's Three Building Blocks

1

Valid Clinical Association (VCA)

Is there a clinically valid relationship between the software's output and the targeted clinical condition? This is the scientific foundation — establishing that what the software measures or predicts is genuinely linked to the disease/condition. VCA is typically established through peer-reviewed literature, clinical studies, or well-accepted physiological principles. Example: For an AF detection ECG app, VCA = the established relationship between specific ECG waveform patterns and atrial fibrillation, documented in cardiology literature.

2

Analytical Validation (AV)

Does the software accurately, reliably, and consistently produce its intended technical output from the input data? This is technical performance testing — demonstrating the algorithm works correctly. For SaMD, this includes: accuracy metrics (sensitivity, specificity, PPV, NPV, AUC), robustness testing across input variability, and performance under edge cases and failure modes. Example: For the AF detection app, AV = testing the algorithm on a large dataset of ECG recordings with known ground truth, measuring sensitivity/specificity, testing performance across different ECG device types, signal qualities, and patient demographics.

3

Clinical Validation (CV)

In the intended clinical environment, with the intended users, for the intended patient population, does the SaMD achieve the intended clinical benefit? This is real-world clinical performance. It requires demonstrating that patients/clinicians using the SaMD achieve better outcomes (or at least equivalent outcomes) compared to the current standard of care. Example: For the AF detection app, CV = a clinical study showing that patients using the app have improved AF detection rates, earlier treatment initiation, or reduced stroke incidence compared to standard monitoring.

All Three Must Be Established — In Sequence

VCA, AV, and CV are not optional — all three must be addressed. Moreover, they are logically sequential: you cannot have AV without VCA (because you need to know what you're measuring is clinically meaningful), and CV without AV (because clinical validity requires technical accuracy). Regulators will reject Clinical Evaluation Reports that skip building blocks or present them out of sequence.

// 04 — EU MDR Requirements

EU MDR Clinical Evaluation Requirements for SaMD

Under EU MDR Annex XIV, Clinical Evaluation is a continuous process — not a one-time pre-market activity. The Clinical Evaluation Report (CER) must be updated throughout the device lifecycle. For SaMD, this means:

Clinical Evaluation Plan (CEP)

Required before starting clinical evaluation. Defines: intended purpose, target population, clinical claims, applicable standards, evaluation methods, and acceptance criteria. The CEP is living document updated with each CER revision.

Clinical Evaluation Report (CER)

Documents all three N41 building blocks with evidence. Must include: literature search methodology and results, clinical data analysis, residual risk assessment, benefit-risk determination. For Class IIb and III devices, Notified Body scrutinises the CER in detail.

Post-Market Clinical Follow-up (PMCF)

Mandatory under Annex XIV Part B. For SaMD: real-world performance data collection, post-market registry participation, customer feedback analysis, literature monitoring. PMCF results feed back into CER updates. Cannot be waived for Class IIa and above.

Summary of Safety and Clinical Performance (SSCP)

Publicly available document required for Class III and implantable devices. For Class III SaMD: must summarise clinical evidence, performance data, and benefit-risk conclusion in lay-accessible language. Published on EUDAMED.

// 05 — Summary

Key Takeaways

Three Blocks, All Mandatory

Valid Clinical Association, Analytical Validation, and Clinical Validation are all required. Each feeds into the next. Missing any one block will result in an incomplete Clinical Evaluation — and likely a major non-conformity in EU audits or an AI request from FDA.

Clinical Evaluation Is Continuous

Under EU MDR, the CER is a living document. Plan your PMCF data collection strategy at the same time as your pre-market clinical evaluation. They share the same evidence framework.

Literature Search Is Not Enough for CV

For Category III/IV SaMD, analytical validation alone will not satisfy Clinical Validation. A clinical study on your specific device in your specific intended patient population is almost always required by regulators.

Representativeness Is Scrutinised

Regulators — especially FDA for AI/ML — closely examine whether your validation dataset represents the intended use population. Single-site, single-demographics validation is consistently flagged. Plan multi-site, diverse-population clinical studies from the start.

Next Step Recommendation

Develop your Clinical Evaluation Plan before starting any validation study. The CEP defines your acceptance criteria — without it, you don't know whether your study results are sufficient. For novel high-risk SaMD, engage FDA via Pre-Submission or a Notified Body via a preliminary review to agree on the clinical evidence standard before committing to an expensive clinical investigation.
