// 01 — Foundation

The Strategic Decision That Determines Everything

Regulatory pathway selection is the most consequential early decision in SaMD development. Choose the wrong pathway and you invest 12–18 months in a submission strategy that FDA will reject. Choose the right pathway and you set up the entire development programme for efficiency — the clinical evidence requirements, software documentation standards, and post-market obligations all flow from this single decision.

For FDA, the three pathways are: 510(k) (substantial equivalence to a predicate), De Novo (novel low-to-moderate risk with no valid predicate), and PMA (Class III, highest risk). The decision tree is sequential — work through it in order.

// 02 — Decision Framework

The FDA Pathway Selection Decision Tree

1

Is the SaMD a Regulated Medical Device? (21 CFR 801, FD&C Act §201(h))

If the software is Clinical Decision Support (CDS) — does it meet the four-criteria exemption under the 21st Century Cures Act? (Non-device CDS: (1) not for serious/critical conditions OR (2) displays basis for recommendation so clinician can independently review OR (3) intended for HCP use AND (4) manufacturer provides list of references.) If all four criteria are met, it is non-device CDS and no premarket submission is required. If any criterion fails, it is SaMD and requires regulation.

2

What FDA Device Class? (21 CFR Part 862–892, Product Code Database)

Identify the FDA device class using the product code database and relevant classification regulations. Class I = general controls only (most exempt from premarket notification). Class II = 510(k) or De Novo. Class III = PMA. SaMD product codes: e.g., QMF (general SaMD), DZO (computer-assisted detection), LLZ (AI-based diagnostic). The product code determines everything downstream including Special Controls.

3

Is There a Valid Predicate for 510(k)?

Search the 510(k) database for cleared devices with (a) the same intended use AND (b) the same or different-but-defensible technological characteristics. If a valid predicate exists: proceed with 510(k). If no valid predicate exists: evaluate De Novo eligibility. Key principle: Do not force a poor predicate match. An NSE determination is permanently documented and signals regulatory risk to investors, partners, and future reviewers.

4

De Novo: Novel + Low-to-Moderate Risk?

De Novo (21 CFR 513(f)(2)) is appropriate when: the device is novel (no valid predicate exists) AND it is low-to-moderate risk (Class I or II appropriate). De Novo creates a new device classification with Special Controls — your cleared device becomes the predicate for future similar devices. Timeline: 12–15 months typical. Requires full safety and effectiveness data — it is not a lightweight 510(k) alternative.

5

PMA: Class III, Novel + High Risk

PMA (21 CFR Part 814) applies when the device is Class III and there is no valid 510(k) or De Novo pathway. PMA requires valid scientific evidence — typically one or more well-controlled clinical trials — that the device is safe and effective. Timeline: 180 days statutory (rarely achieved) — typical 24–36+ months. Cost: $5–20M+ in clinical and regulatory expenses. Consider whether design modifications could reduce risk classification before committing to PMA.

// 04 — EU Parallel Strategy

Running FDA and EU Pathways in Parallel

Most commercial SaMD manufacturers need both FDA clearance and EU CE marking. These pathways are independent — there is no mutual recognition between them. However, a well-structured development programme can generate evidence that satisfies both simultaneously, reducing duplicated effort.

Shared Evidence Strategy

Clinical validation data (IMDRF N41 Clinical Validation) satisfies both FDA's performance testing requirements and EU MDR's Clinical Evaluation requirements. Design your clinical study protocol to address both regulators' data needs from a single study. This is the highest-leverage efficiency in dual-market SaMD development.

IEC 62304 Documentation — Universal

IEC 62304-compliant software documentation is accepted by both FDA and EU MDR. One set of documentation, built to the standard, serves both submissions. The only differences are jurisdiction-specific formatting and cross-references — the underlying technical content is identical.

Timeline Misalignment

FDA 510(k) (120-180 days) is significantly faster than EU MDR CE marking for Class IIb/III (18-36 months). Plan for US market entry first, using FDA clearance as a signal of credibility for the EU submission. US real-world performance data can support EU PMCF requirements.

AI Act Dual Compliance (2026)

From August 2, 2026, high-risk AI SaMD must comply with both EU MDR and the EU AI Act. CE marking under MDR does not satisfy AI Act obligations. If your device is AI-based, build your technical documentation to address both regulatory frameworks from the start. Module 03 of the AI/ML domain covers this in detail.

// 05 — Summary

Key Takeaways

Pathway First, Documentation Second

Never start writing a submission until you have confirmed your pathway through the decision tree. The pathway determines what evidence is required — building evidence before pathway confirmation wastes resources.

Pre-Submission Is Free Insurance

FDA's Pre-Submission (Q-Sub) program is free and provides written FDA feedback on your pathway choice, predicate strategy, and testing approach. For any novel or complex SaMD, a Pre-Sub is the single most important risk-mitigation tool available.

De Novo Creates Your Own Predicate

A successful De Novo determination creates a new device type with Special Controls — and your cleared device becomes the predicate for all future similar devices. This is a significant market advantage: you define the regulatory standard that competitors must meet.

Design to Pathway

If PMA is looking inevitable, evaluate whether design modifications — limiting intended use, adding human oversight requirements, restricting patient population — could reduce risk sufficiently for De Novo or 510(k). This design-to-pathway thinking can save years of development time and tens of millions in clinical study costs.
